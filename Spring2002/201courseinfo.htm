COURSE OBJECTIVES:
1) To improve your ability to understand, speak, read, and write French;
2) To expose you to important aspects of French and Francophone culture;
3) to prepare you for advanced coursework in French, and for study, work, or travel in French-speaking settings.

In order to accomplish these goals, we must use French as the principal language of the classroom. Although most of you will be taking this course as a requirement, foreign language will enhance your career opportunities, whether in the sciences, social sciences, arts, or humanities. Recruiters in business, banking, government, education, the media, and industry are increasingly looking for people with foreign-language abilities.

In addition to enhancing your professional opportunities, studying French can have other significant benefits. First, it can provide the chance for meaningful travel to France or the Francophone world (Québec, a number of Caribbean islands, many African countries, Belgium, Switzerland, Tahiti, etc.) Second, in learning about the differences and similarities of another culture, you achieve a new perspective on your own. As Benjamin Franklin--who lived in Paris for several years--once said: “It is only possible to truly understand one’s own country by understanding another.”

PLACEMENT AND PREREQUISITES: Successful completion of FLF 102 at NCSU, transfer credit of its equivalent at another university, or placement into FLF 201 on the NCSU Placement Test. RESPONSIBLITIES: I will do my best to make the class atmosphere creative, lively, and accepting of your best efforts. In turn, you too have some responsibilities....

1) Prepare assignments before coming to class so we can get the most out of our classtime
together. Assignments are to be completed as indicated on the syllabus. It is nearly impossible to “cram” for this course and do well. Learning a language is like building a house of cards: each one must be well in place before adding the next. I urge you to spend at least 30 minutes on French per day, rather than studying all at once before exams. Regular repetition of small quantities of information is the most successful method for learning a foreign language.

2) Participate actively in each lesson. Mentally answer each and every question when a classmate is responding. Class participation and homework make up 25% of your grade. The more you participate, the better your grade will be, and the better your French will become. Your class grade reflects your performance in class (attendance, knowledge of daily lesson, volunteering answers, asking questions, class reflections). You should work constantly with the language lab tapes. Play them on your car or home stereo; even if you’re not always actively listening, it will help your proficiency. You will often have to listen to the tapes more than once, so don’t get discouraged if you cannot understand every word the first time. The tape exercises will get easier as the semester progresses, if you make the effort. Take the tape work seriously — it will help both your proficiency and your grade (there is a listening component on exams).

3) Take risks. Speak out. Don’t be afraid to make mistakes. I am here to help. The more you speak, the more you will get out of the class.

4) Speak French as much as possible in the classroom. To this end, the following expressions will be helpful:

Répétez, s’il vous plaît. Repeat, please
Comment dit-on “....” en français? How do you say “....” in French?
Que veut dire “....”? What does “....” mean?
Qu’est-ce que c’est? What’s that?
Je ne sais pas. I don’t know.
Je ne comprends pas. I don’t understand.
J’ai une question. I have a question.

5) Bring textbook to class every day. This is essential. You should also purchase a decent quality
French/English-English/French dictionary. While not required, it will help you find words not
explained in the textbook.

6) Be playful and participate in games, songs, skits, and conversations.

7) Take notes in class whenever appropriate. Buy 3x5 notecards to make personalized verb charts and other study aids. Study together in pairs or groups outside of class.

8) Use French outside of class. See French movies in theaters or on video (Blockbuster, North American Video, and the Media Center in D.H. Hill Library have great French film collections). Talk to your roomates and friends in French. Listen to French CDs and visit French web sites. Go to cultural events sponsored by organizations such as the Triangle’s Alliance Française (I will announce these in class). You are also welcome to join the French Club during the academic year, to get extra exposure to the language, and to meet French and Francophone students. You can earn extra credit by turning in commentaries on French-related films, art exhibits, or other cultural experiences.

I look forward to a pleasant and productive semester! Don't hesitate to ask me for help and assistance in person or by email.
