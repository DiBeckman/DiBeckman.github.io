WHAT TO CONSIDER WHEN LOOKING AT A WORK OF ART

There are four basic parts to an analysis:

1. Description…what you see and know about the work

• What is its size?
• What materials or media are employed?
• Were any special techniques used to make this work?
• What is the date of the work’s creation? Where was it created?
• Where is it now? Who owns it?
• What is the title of the work?
• Is it a landscape, portrait, still-life, genre, religious, history, mythological work?
• What is the subject matter or theme? (e.g. a statue of Apollo, a crucifixion, a church)
• Describe this theme if it has a textual source (e.g. Apollo is the mythical god of the sun)
• Take an inventory of what you see. List everything that is in front of you.
• Describe the basic imagery (e.g. there are 3 female figures standing on a hill with the sun setting behind them)
• Who is the artist or architect? When & where did they live/work?
• What do you know about the artist?
• At what point in the artist’s career was the work produced?

2. Formal Analysis…the design of the work (how is it constructed?)

For two-dimensional works:
• What element or elements seem to dominate?
• Is color important?
• What colors are used? Warm (red-yellow-orange) or cool (violet-green-blue)?
• How are the colors arranged?
• Does the artist change the value and intensity of the colors?
• Do the colors affect one another? Do the colors advance and recede?
• Is line an important element?
• Are forms organized on a flat plane or are they set deep into space? How does the artist achieve this? (overlapping, diminution, perspective?)
• How is depth created? Is it shallow or deep?
• Describe the texture (smooth, shiny, matte, rough). How would you describe the brushwork?
• Is there the illusion of texture? Do the materials create actual texture?
• What shapes predominate?
• How is the composition balanced, asymmetrically or symmetrically?
• Is there a focal point? How is it created?
• Are there elements of the work that repeat? What does the repetition do to the arrangement?
• What elements are in contrast? How does this contrast affect the composition?

Additional points of consideration for three-dimensional works:
• Is the piece designed to be seen in the round or from a specific point or points?
• Is it part of a larger whole, such as a sculpture on a building or monument?
• How does light affect how it is seen?
• Is it an open or closed form?

3. Interpretation: What does the work mean?
• What does the work “say” to you personally?
• What is your subjective reaction to the work of art?
• How has the artist used the elements of composition (line, color, shape, texture, space) to create meaning or content?
• What ideas does it put forth?
• Does the imagery present an allegorical story?
• Are symbols used? Are there metaphors? Discover the iconography of the work.
• What is the function of this work of art? Who might the audience or buyer be for this piece?
• What connection do you see between subject matter and the techniques used?
• What is the cultural meaning and context of the work?
• How does the work reflect the social, political, aesthetic, or cultural ideas of its time?
• How do others react to this work? How do they interpret it?

4. Conclusion: evaluate the work in terms of other historical models
• How does the piece compare with other works by this artist?
• If it is described as a particular style, what about it distinguishes the style? (e.g. what makes a Monet painting an Impressionist painting?)
