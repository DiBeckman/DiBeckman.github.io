Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Wednesday, February 12

Finish reading Madame Bovary 1215-1249. Section 1 will meet as usual. Wednesday's lecture on Madame Bovary is here.

Writing assignments:

  1. Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Book III?
  2. Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

    FORMALIST CRITICISM:

    • How do the names of individual characters tie into the main messages within Madame Bovary?  Are the names meant to hold hidden significance (Leon could be lion, Justin could be justice, Rodolphe could be rodent, etc.)? 
    • How does Flaubert's use of free indirect discourse reinforce the overall meaning of the story(is it simply stylistic or does it enhance the meaning)? 
    • Flaubert alters the tempo throughout Madame Bovary, by briefly mentioning certain events and going into great length on others. What is the purpose of this and how does this benefit the story?
    • Does Emma's taste in literature have a specific connection to the plot of Madame Bovary?  If so, what is the significance of these allusions and how do they enhance the plot?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • How do economic conditions determine Emma's actions in Madame Bovary?
    • Which social class is Flaubert frustrated with and how does he portray this in the story?
    • In Madame Bovary, who represents the proletariat social class and who represents the bourgeoisie? What affect do these social classes have on the characters' views on themselves in society?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • Would Emma still be existentially bored and frustrated if she were a man? Would her manner of dealing with her problems change in any way?
    • What was the level of freedom for women in Flaubert's day? Were they lived in more restricted life or had a choice to make decisions?
      Were they allowed to complain about their life or did they just have to deal with it?
    • Emma seems to blame her unhappiness, and inability to achieve her romantic ideals, on the constraints that are put on women. She harbors a resentment towards the freedom she perceives men to have that women do not, and this is a source of much of her frustration and enhanced depression/bipolar like symptoms. It is for this reason she wanted to have a son and not a daughter. Do you think that if she had given birth to a son, instead of a daughter, she would have been a more loving mother? 
    • How do the societal expectations and differences in education between men and women at the time influence the opportunities of the characters 
      in the novel to fulfill their goals and desires? How does Flaubert showcase the differences in opportunities for men and women through Emma's marriage and affairs?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • How may have Emma Bovary’s life been different if her story had taken place in one hundred years later (1950/1960’s) and how different would it have been if it had taken place current day, as opposed to the 1800's? (Dr. B adds: For one person's answer, read The Kugelmass Episode by Woody Allen about Emma Bovary arriving in New York City in 1977. )
    • What would Emma do/How would she react if she was suddenly moved down (or up) a class level? That is to say, if she were to become very very poor, or very very rich and influential instead of her current middle class life?
    • Suppose you were to make a movie production of Madame Bovary, set in contemporary USA (think Baz Luhrmann's Romeo + Juliet http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0117509/ with Leonardo DiCaprio, but not to a word-for-word rewrite.) What would be the most interesting social, historical, or economic hurdle to work around to make your production both true to the writing and believable to the viewer? Would something in particular be impossible to work around? Choose your favorite character and describe their modern day job and tell us what they would be wearing when first seen on camera. Choose your favorite setting (perhaps a house, the golden lion, or Homais' workshop) and describe how you imagined it when reading Madame Bovary and how you would create the set on the film.
    • Does Part III of the novel reflect anything from the author's life history that may have influenced this piece?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • In Madame Bovary, what examples from the text suggest that Emma may actually be suffering from a mental illness? Which mental illness do you think this is and why? 
    • What does the author express about desire? How does Flaubert express desire in the text through characters like Emma and Leon?
    • Why was Emma so unsuccessful in marriage? Was she fated to be unsuccessful because of her obsession with emotions evidenced from a young age, or did her infatuation with romantic novels set her up for failure?
    • Why does Emma feel the need to be married and why does her heart decide to take a different turn in love? What symbols portray her actions when Emma falls for another man? Is this betrayal against Charles, or is this a new love that is being shed on Emma that she was originally looking for?
    • why does Charles suddenly start to visualize and witness Emma in a way he has never seen any other woman before? Charles goes on describing her body parts, her hair, fingernails, etc. Is there an environmental aspect that plays a role in this? How is this going to change the emotions of the characters later on in the story when the truth gets exposed?
    • After learning his personal background and studying the way Gustave Flaubert puts parts of his own personality into his writing, do any of the characters or aspects of characters appear to be constructed around himself or his own life?
    • Would Emma be satisfied if she were to elope with Monsieur Leon? Or would Emma still have the desire to sate her needs for her fantasy novel life? If so, could Monsieur Leon handle Emma's desire, or is there a greater man for her?

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • Who was Madame Bovary's iintended audience? To what extent are you similar or dissimilar to that audience?
    • How does Emma's actions and dialogue affect your understanding of reality?
    • How would you identify and interpret Charles' character throughout his relationship with Emma?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • How do the opposite settings of the countryside, and the city (two places Emma lived in) compare, symbolize, and/or relate to Emma and Charles's relationship?  How does this relate to Emma's emotions and mixed feelings throughout the book?
    • In what way(s) does Emma's environment contribute to her desires?
    • What significance do you think nature has had on Madame Bovary? Does she yearn for nature like the romantic poets or during the Enlightenment period? Does the fact that she grew up surrounded by nature show anything about how she wanted to live life?
    • Gustave Flaubert is metaphor heavy when it comes to comparing people with animals, farm animals in particular. How does the extended metaphor, people are like farm animals, affect the theme(s) of Madame Bovary?
    • How do Emma's dreams of her perfect romantic environment contrast with her real "dull countryside, imbecile petty bourgeois, life in its ordinariness" environment?

    QUEER THEORY:

    • How does Madame Bovary's intimate relationships with men differ from her neutral male relationships in the story?
    • How might Emma's feeling of sexuality be different than Charle's and how does this difference affect their relationship and the work as a whole?

Class 33 (Kafka 2) due Wednesday, April 2

Now answer your own question, or another student's (see below), based on the whole story. In other words, how might we interpret the Metamorphosis through the lens of Formalist, Marxist, Feminist… criticism? Think especially about the violin scene, the death scene and the final paragraph.

For fun, check out: Which character are you in The Metamorphosis?

"We are all sentenced to solitary confinement inside our own skins, for life." Tennessee Williams. Recommended reading: two articles relate to what I'll call The Gerasim Effect—The United States holds tens of thousands of inmates in long-term solitary confinement. Is this torture? by Atul Gawande and Comforter and Comforted in an Unfolding Mystery by Nell Burger Kirst. These articles could lead to a potential paper topic.

FORMALIST CRITICISM:

  • How does the form of the story relate to its meaning?
  • Why does the first sentence contain what could be described as the climax of the story?
  • What is the significance of the "monstrous vermin" metaphor? What could it represent? What is its purpose?

SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

Marxist Criticism:

  • How does the father's failure at business affect Gregor's mindset in the beginning of the novella?
  • What are the social statuses that Kafka relates to as sociological criticism?
  • What social class does Gregor's family fall into and how does Gregor play a role in defining that class?
  • How much of an impact does the Samsa family's financial situation play into the story?
  • The longer Gregor is a vermin, how do his ideological values change? How do his goals in life change with regards to his family?

Feminist Criticism:

  • If Gregor had a brother instead of a sister, would his brother have been responsible for caring for Gregor or was this simply her responsibility because she was a female?
  • What dies Gregor's preoccupation with the photo of the woman in furs say about how he, and Kafka, view women during this period?
  • How does the first half of the story depict the "typical woman" role in society and what characteristics are the women given?

CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

  • How does Metamorphosis reflect the period in which it was created?
  • Some argue that The Metamorphosis portrays the soldier’s feelings post World War I.  How does Gregor’s transformation and qualities directly pertain to the war and society’s response to it?
  • How might this story been affected if it was written in the present day as opposed to 1915? If the story were made in a movie then versus now, would the plot be diminished by the gory and severe special effects used in movies today or would it be enhanced? Also, if it was made into a movie, would that diminish the effect of creating your own mental image of Gregor as the vermin or enhance it?
  • In Part I of The Metamorphosis, there is a focus on Gregor’s job as a traveling salesman and the perception that people have about him based on this career; how does this reflect the culture of the time period? Can you think of a modern job that would raise the same concerns about a person?

PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

  • From a Psychological standpoint, how would you explain his desire toward the lady with a fur coat and a boa in the frame that he is so attached to?
  • How does Gregor's personality change after the metamorphosis and how does it stay the same?
  • Gregor, after realizing his physical change and throughout Metamorphosis, undergoes social and psychological change as well. What are these changes and do you think these changes are reflections of what Kafka has experienced in his own life?
  • How is each character coping with the transformation of Gregor's physical state?
  • When Gregor changes into a monstrous vermin, what is the psychological state of the family and how does it change?
  • What is the great psychological disconnect that Gregor is experiencing during this part of the story? Explain the conflict and provide examples.
  • Is the behavior of the family in “Metamorphosis” after Gregor woke up as a vermin reflective of Kafka’s personal relationship (or lack thereof) with his father? How?
  • How does Gregor's perspective compare to what is going on around him?

READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

  • While reading Metamorphosis, were there any instances in which you find yourself relating to Gregor or any of this family members?
  • Do you think that the people's reactions were justified when they learned of Gregor's sudden alteration of human life to a monstrous vermin? Would you have reacted the same way as those around Gregor?
  • Why is there no reason given for his transformation?

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

  • Does the lack of setting identification and explanation of the environment affect the reader's comprehension of the story? Why or why not?
  • How does Gregory's physical transformation mimic the claustrophobic environment he is bound to in the story?
  • Why do you think that Kafka chose to transform Gregor into a vermin instead of some other creature or something else from nature? Would this work have the same effect if Kafka had not made Gregor a vermin and titled the story Metamorphosis?
  • How did Gregory's metamorphosis change the world around him?
  • How did Gregory's surroundings affect his life after the metamorphosis?

QUEER THEORY:

  • How does the way Gregor's family treats him after he becomes a vermin similar to the way family and friends react to someone after they admit to being homosexual?
  • What object(s) in Gregor's apartment does he have a sensual attachment to?
