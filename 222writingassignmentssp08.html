Here are two additional sets of critical questions you may choose to address. They were written by a former student, Garry Pigg (Fall, 2005)

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM

1. How do the environmental/ecological conditions within the setting of the work affect the language, mood, tone, and theme?
2. How does the use of the word "nature" in the work reflect the current scientific beliefs of the time and how does that affect the characters, plot, themes?
3. How does the relationship between man/woman and his/her environment--economically, agriculturally, botanically, socially--affect the characters, language, theme, plot, and tone? i.e. do the characters have to hunt and gather, farm, trade, live in city, country?
4. How do plants, animals, and their conditions in the environment affect the character's livelihood, the plot, the author's tone, the moods, the theme? i.e. the effects of individual species such as cows, camels, horses,goats, bananas, sugar, cotton, corn, potatoes, fungi, bacteria,
viruses; and ambient conditions such as drought, flooding, pollution, climate change, introduction of invasive species, and famine.

QUEER THEORY

1. How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plot, and themes?
2. How does sexual identity affect the characters? How do they deal with others and their own sexual deviance or breaking of social norms?
3. How does homosexuality or bisexuality play a role in the characters' lives, the tone, mood, theme, and plot of the work?
4. What role does sexuality play in the work? How does it affect the use of language? Does it represent love and beauty or marriage and
reproduction?
5. How does religion affect the tendencies of the characters to express or not express themselves sexually?
6. How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sexuality to define "others"? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism? Does it hurt or help racism, discrimination in general, and civil rights? What is the connection between sexuality, race, gender, and social class in the work(s)?

Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, February 15
Madame Bovary 1215-1249 Shannon: Chapter 5
1) Finish your character sketch to turn in. 2) Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Web CT posts or the idea for Paper 1.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM
-Gustave Flaubert changes the narrative perspective/whose eyes the reader sees through many times through the novel. Is this helpful or harmful?
-How does Flaubert's use of indirect discourse and detached narration provide a way for him to express his opinions on each of the characters as well as on the manners and customs of provincial life?
-The color blue appears numerous times in almost every chapter. How does the use of this color in descriptions relate to the overall themes of love and despair?
-How do Emma's actions throughout Madame Bovary contribute to the overall meaning of the book?
-How do Rodolphe and Leon affect the overall plot of Madame Bovary?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
  Marxist Criticism
-Throughtout the novel, Emma tries to move up in social status from the rurual middle class to the aristocracy. What steps does Emma take to elevate herself above her current position to reach the artistocracy? What does this say about Flaubert's ideas on social class position?
-It seem like even in the old days people knew where they stood in society in terms of wealth. However, in Madame Bovary even though Charles is a doctor, he is looked upon numerous times as a peasant. Why did Flaubert do this?
-In Madame Bovary is Marxism used to separate the classes of people, as in the contrast between the wet nurse and the way Madame Bovary live. However, are these classes going to be continued through the story or will the struggle for a classless society succeed?
-How does the author use the characters Emma and Charles to criticize the middle class?
-Are any or all ofthe characters aware of their social class? If so, what evidence points to their knowledge? How do they react to their standings?
-How does Flaubert distinguish between social classes in Madame Bovary-what are the references to the bourgeois class?
-Monsieur Lheureux and his agressive lending tactics to persons unable to afford the loans he so aggressively markets to them coincides with today's poor economic conditions relative to mortgage brokers' lucrative offers to potential homebuyers. Do you believe that the ramifications occuring to a person who is "over their head" financially is because of his/her own ignorance and desire to have more in this consumer-driven society OR should there be stricter moral and economic regulations put forth on the lenders because they are, in sense, "taking advantage of the senseless"?

  Feminist Criticism
-In the past, women have often been portrayed as dolls used for show and to take care of men. Do you think that the way Emma dresses and dreams of being in a higher social class coincides with this view? Do you think Emma is falling into the stereotype and not realizing it?
-How are women leading their lives? Do they do as they want or do they do as they are told?
-Does the relationship between Rodolphe and Emma reveal differing opinions between love/relationships of men and women? If so, is the author providing his views or criticism of how women and men differ in viewing love?
-Does it both Emma that Charles is the only source of income in the house? Does she wish she had something to do with her time?
-Emma has a husband who loves her and a new baby but it seems that she's not happy with what she's got and wants more. Provide examples from the text that prove that Emma wants more in her life than living in Yonville with her husband and baby.
-How are women portrayed in the story? What's their gender role? How do they react to it?
-Emma seems very smart and treated with respect. What is her unhappiness? Why is she unhappy after being treated nicely by Leon? She is a woman and maybe should have less say and rights during this time period.

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM
-How would Madame Bovary be different if Flaubert wrote in the Romantic Period?
-How can the cultural and historical settings of Madame Bovary, which takes place in 1840s France, be related to modern readers?
How does the rise of the bourgeoisie and their values fit into the story and the way of life of the characters in it?
-How does the role of history affect the realism in this novel, and how is it different from the Romantic period?
-How did Gustave Flaubert use realism and themes from the 19th century in his story Madame Bovary?
- Do you think the sense of family values and the sacredness of marriage has changed from the time of this story to now?
-How do the mannerisms and language style of the characters compare to the time period for which Madame Bovary was written in as well as how do they compare to the modern mannerisms and language style of the United States?
-What cultural roles were addressed in Madame Bovary and in what context were they presented? Was culture an influence and in what ways is it reflected in the story?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-How and what may be the cause of each character's delusions? What could be the root cause of it?
-Flaubert provides a sense of insecurity in Madame Bovary through Emma's character. Is this insecurity reflected in Emma reflective of Flaubert's feelings or experiences in life? What is Flaubert thinking when he continues to make Emma's life a miserable happiness?
-How does Madame Bovary reflect Flaubert's personal psychology?
-What does Emma's behavior/emotions reveal about kind of person she is/ personality she has? What does Charles' behavior/emotion or lack thereof, reveal about his personality?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM
-What are your feelings towards Emma? Do you sympathize with her or are you annoyed by her actions? Does your personality type influence the way you feel about Emma or do you know someone like Emma?
-Who do you think is repsonsible for the many misfortunes that occur in the story: Emma or Charles? Why do you think they are to be blamed?
-Emma has many relationships throughout the novel. Compare and contrast the relationships that Emma has with Charles, Leon and Rodophe. When does she meet them, how does she act towards them? Also, what are the differences in all the men that she is with in this novel? In the end, who do you think she loves the most?
-Obviously, Madame Bovary was written before the time of medical diagnosis for mental disorders such as depression, bipolar disorder, etc. Do you think that more people today can relate to Emma and her unhappy state? Does her behavior make more sense and appear more common to audiences of today rather than in the past? If so, is it easier for intended audiences of today to relate to Emma and understand her complex personality?
-In general, do you think a lot of people's lives nowadays are goverened by the idea the grass is always greener on the other side? In terms of story, are the some people who dream up a better life with another person and then commit adultery because they are unhappy with the life they lead? If there are people like this in our world, which I know there are, does this in turn affect the way these people interpret the story versus the way other people who don't believe that see this story?

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-How is Madame Bovary affected by the environment of the city compared to the environment of the country? How does her mood, her look, and her well-being change in each environment? And does the environment and her lovers' characters correlate with the environment that she has relations with them?
-Why does the nature and the environment around Yonville seem to affect Leon and Emma so negatively?
-How does the contrast between the environments Emma experiences affect her overall attitude, as well as the general tone of the novel? Consider soical, economic, and agricultural environments and also how Emma's attitude affects other characters throughout the story.

• QUEER THEORY
-How does Madame Bovary's overwhelming sexuality and breaking of the social norm of marriage affect the other characters of the story?
-How does Flaubert use the characters in Madame Bovary to portray his idea of the restrictions of gender roles? Emma seems ready to prostitute herself to pay off her debts. Was he trying to suggest that females must use their bodies to get anywhere in life?
-"There is no lonelier woman than one who degrades her gender by using it to get special favors." How does this quote relate to Emma Bovary's character? What type of stereotypes exist about women and how does Emma's character epitomize the stereotypes?
-Given the fact that Emma is very romantic and expresses Romantic thoughts, how do her sexual thoughts and emotions affect other characters and herself? Why is she always having these sexual thoughts?
-Is there a connection bewteen the sexual descriptions of Emma and her romantic fantasies?
-Does Emma reading her romantic novels influence the way she reacts to men when they are trying to seduce her?
-Does Rodolphe's character reflect cultural feelings toward sex?

2) Also: "The Oedipus complex in Freudian psychoanalysis refers to stage of psychosexual development in childhood where children of both sexes regard their father as an adversary and competitor for the exclusive love of their mother. The name of the complex derives from the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unwittingly kills his father Laius and marries his mother Jocasta.

Freud considered the successful resolution of the Oedipus complex to be key to the development of gender roles and identity, with boys and girls resolving the conflicts differently as a result of castration anxiety and penis envy. Freud also held that the unsuccessful resolution of the Oedipus complex could result in neurosis and homosexuality.… Although common usage refers to 'suffering from an Oedipus complex', psychoanalysis does not consider the complex a pathology, but instead a perfectly normal stage that all children go through. Oedipal desires are thought to remain heavily repressed and unconsciously in the minds of all functioning adults."
