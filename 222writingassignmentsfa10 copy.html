Here are two additional, more modern, sets of critical questions you may choose to address. They were written by a former student, Garry Pigg (Fall, 2005)

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
1. How do the environmental/ecological conditions within the setting of the work affect the language, mood, tone, and theme?
2. How does the use of the word "nature" in the work reflect the current scientific beliefs of the time and how does that affect the characters, plot, themes?
3. How does the relationship between man/woman and his/her environment--economically, agriculturally, botanically, socially--affect the characters, language, theme, plot, and tone? i.e. do the characters have to hunt and gather, farm, trade, live in city, country?
4. How do plants, animals, and their conditions in the environment affect the character's livelihood, the plot, the author's tone, the moods, the theme? i.e. the effects of individual species such as cows, camels, horses, goats, bananas, sugar, cotton, corn, potatoes, fungi, bacteria,
viruses; and ambient conditions such as drought, flooding, pollution, climate change, introduction of invasive species, and famine.

QUEER THEORY:

1. How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plot, and themes?
2. How does sexual identity affect the characters? How do they deal with others and their own sexual deviance or breaking of social norms?
3. How does homosexuality or bisexuality play a role in the characters' lives, the tone, mood, theme, and plot of the work?
4. What role does sexuality play in the work? How does it affect the use of language? Does it represent love and beauty or marriage and
reproduction?
5. How does religion affect the tendencies of the characters to express or not express themselves sexually?
6. How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sexuality to define "others"? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism? Does it hurt or help racism, discrimination in general, and civil rights? What is the connection between sexuality, race, gender, and social class in the work(s)?

Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, September 24
Madame Bovary 1215-1249

1) Finish your character sketch to turn in. 2) Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM:
- How does Emma's Romantic nature conflict/contribute to the story's Realist nature?
- How does Flaubert use descriptions and writing style to make the reader feel the same way that the character feels?
- How does Emma's insatiable desire for passionate affairs relate to her Romantic sensibility?
- Would the author be successful as a Romantic author?
- Is the work's major organizing principle Flaubert's goal of "writing a book about nothing"?
- What does the title of the work reflect about the story?


• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
  Marxist Criticism:
- How do Emma's feelings towards her class status affect the decisions she chooses to make throughout the novel? Why do you think Flaubert chooses to portray Emma like this, but allows Charles to be content with their status?
- How does Flaubert portray social class in Madame Bovary and how does it show his own feelings about class?
- If Emma was not satisfied with her social status before she married Charles, when why she did marry a not romantic man that is also not wealthy?
- How does the economy, the amount of money available to characters and their social class affect their lives and mentality?
- If the characters' social classes were switched, how different would the story be?


  Feminist Criticism:
- What are the different roles and duties between men and women in Madame Bovary? eg: Charles, the doctor; Homais, pharmacist; Rouault, farmer. Do women take on any duties or have to work? Does this depend on class structure? eg Wet nurse, Félicité.
- How are women's desires/dreams different from men's in Madame Bovary?
- The way that women are portrayed seems to be that they are dreamers and naive? Does that have to do with the time period and the way they seemed back then?
- Through the creation of Emma's character, is Flaubert trying to hinder feminism by trying to show how the female sex cannot fulfill her role efficiently because she is distracted by passion and material goods?
- In what ways does Flaubert continuously build a theme of discontent in women throughout Madame Bovary?
- How do Emma's romantic ideas influence the plot of the story?
- What does this work tell us about the author's view of women during this time?
- Do Emma Bovary's affairs show the outcome of restrictions in this time period? Is it her way of feeling free?


• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:
-What significance does the cathedral and the priest trying to show Emma and Leon every last part of it have in the story? What commentary is the author trying to get across by including it?
- Why does Madame Bovary love the romantic period so much, while this novel was written during the realist period?
- What role does the Industrial Revolution play in the society in which Madame Bovary lives?
- How does culture affect the feelings/ideas of the characters in the story?
- How do Emma's relationships with others and reactions to situations reflect the view of Romanticism in the era of Realism?
- Other than French culture, are there any other cultures that helped Gustave write this story?
- What kind of unspoken rules/social behaviors lead characters to their actions? For example: Emma's high class behavior etc.


• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
- Do any of the characters show symptoms of any psychological disorders? If so, why would the author choose this as an explanation for their actions, feelings, and characteristics?
- Does Emma's inablity to be happy relate to Flaubert's psychological feelings?
- What does Charles' mother's emotions toward Emma reveal about her psychological state?
- In Madame Bovary, Emma is portrayed as a discontented character with wildly changing emotions. She could even be described as psychologically unstable. How does Flaubert's writing style change with Emma's psychological state? How do these shifts affect the flow of the novel?
- What is the author's stance, if any, on the manipulation of people and the effect it has on their psychology?
- Is Emma actually aware of her actions and the effect that they'll have on people around her? Or are her actions unconscious? If they are unconscious, if they became conscious, would she change her ways or continue on?
- Who has the dominant personality between Charles and Emma? Support your answer with examples from the text.

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:
- How do Madame Bovary's romantic desires and her situation affect/relate to your own current desires and dreams?
- After having read Madame Bovary, do you see your own desires and dreams in a different light?
- How would you describe Emma's feelings toward Berthe and how might this have been different if Berthe had been a boy?
- In what way(s) did Flaubert write Madame Bovary to connect to the reader's real life experiences?
- How do you respond to Charles' failure to fix Hippolyte's clubfoot in Book II, Chapter ii, considering what happened to Hippolyte post operation and Madame Bovary's reaction to it all?
- If you have ever experienced dissatification with life, are you able to feel more connected with Emma? Why or why not?

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
- How does nature enhance the relationship of the characters in the scene or the mood of the scene?
- How does the author personify the environmnet to enhance the characters' traits? Espcially with Emma and Léon? Does the author's tone change? His writing style?

• QUEER THEORY:
- Is Emma's infidelity due to her displeasure with Charles or to her displeasure with her social standing?
- Do you think that since Emma knows Leon, Charles, and Justin are all in love with her that it is affecting her emotions? Is having too many lovers making her become more depressed/crazy?
- How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plots, and themes in Madame Bovary?
- How are Emma's feelings about her sexuality expressed through other characters in the work? What does this tell us about the cultural feelings toward sex?

For quotations from the text, keep in mind that they are the evidence you need to provide to prove your point. You will usually use at least one in each of body paragraphs. In order to integrate quotes appropriately, be sure to introduce the quote by making the point the quote illustrates, include the quote (usually 3-5 lines of text), and then tie the quote back into your argument with a concluding sentence: "…remind your reader that these passages are there to prove a point. A conclusion is very important to a fully developed paper. Have you've answered a key question in the conclusion to your paper: "So what?" I will be asking the same question when I read your paper.
