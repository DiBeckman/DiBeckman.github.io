A.Word.A.Day
with Anu Garg

Tomorrow Barack Obama will become president of the US, and not a moment too soon. This week we'll feature words from Obama, words from his books, speeches, and interviews.

Unlike most politicians, who hire ghostwriters, Obama writes his own books. He's a gifted writer. Reading his words you can see his thought process. He's not one who sees the globe in black and white. He has lived outside the US and has been exposed to other cultures. He realizes that just because someone has a different set of beliefs, just because someone looks different, doesn't mean he's wrong -- sometimes there can be two ways to do something and both can be right.

Obama is to be commended for his accomplishments. We've come a long way in this country. But we still have far to go before we can call ourselves truly unbiased. Real progress will be when any capable person can have a fair chance at winning the highest office, even someone who happens to be, say, a black gay vegan atheist woman.

Anything is possible... but don't hold your breath.

Here are two additional, more modern, sets of critical questions you may choose to address. They were written by a former student, Garry Pigg (Fall, 2005)

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM

1. How do the environmental/ecological conditions within the setting of the work affect the language, mood, tone, and theme?
2. How does the use of the word "nature" in the work reflect the current scientific beliefs of the time and how does that affect the characters, plot, themes?
3. How does the relationship between man/woman and his/her environment--economically, agriculturally, botanically, socially--affect the characters, language, theme, plot, and tone? i.e. do the characters have to hunt and gather, farm, trade, live in city, country?
4. How do plants, animals, and their conditions in the environment affect the character's livelihood, the plot, the author's tone, the moods, the theme? i.e. the effects of individual species such as cows, camels, horses,goats, bananas, sugar, cotton, corn, potatoes, fungi, bacteria,
viruses; and ambient conditions such as drought, flooding, pollution, climate change, introduction of invasive species, and famine.

QUEER THEORY

1. How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plot, and themes?
2. How does sexual identity affect the characters? How do they deal with others and their own sexual deviance or breaking of social norms?
3. How does homosexuality or bisexuality play a role in the characters' lives, the tone, mood, theme, and plot of the work?
4. What role does sexuality play in the work? How does it affect the use of language? Does it represent love and beauty or marriage and
reproduction?
5. How does religion affect the tendencies of the characters to express or not express themselves sexually?
6. How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sexuality to define "others"? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism? Does it hurt or help racism, discrimination in general, and civil rights? What is the connection between sexuality, race, gender, and social class in the work(s)?

Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, February 13
Madame Bovary 1215-1249 Shannon: Chapter 5

1) Finish your character sketch to turn in. 2) Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Web CT posts or the idea for Paper 1.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM
-In Madame Bovary, we encounter a series of events which affect the lives of the characters. What are the different events that affect Emma's life throughout the story?
-How have the changes in setting throughout the story affected the main characters, Charles and Emma? Include the condition of their relationship, their attitudes towards one another, their overall attitudes, etc.
-What is Lheureux's association with Emma's death?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
Marxist Criticism:
-Were the actions of Lheureux a demonstation of the brutality and excess of the bourgeoisie? Is this evidence that the bourgeois class oppresses not only the proletariat but also their own?
-If the setting of Madame Bovary focused on an ideal Marxist/Socialist society, all other things remaining equal, how might the tone and overreaching ideals of the story differ when framed from the philosophical standpoint of utilitarianism? For simplicity, utilitarianism can be defined as the attempt to maximize the cumulative happiness or satisfaction of all members of society where no individual's opinion may have a weighted difference from any other individual.
-Which elements of the higher social class does Emma admire?
-In what way does Flaubert associate the social class of the people in Madame Bovary with the actual presence of class they have in their soul?
-Do the people of Yonville treat Emma differently because of how she dresses and her social status? Does social class really affect the people of Yonville's opinions on one another?
-How do you think it would affect the story if Emma had been a farmer? Would Rodolphe and Léon still have fallen in love with her? Or what if she married a man from the ball? Would she have cheated?

Feminist Criticism:
- Is Emma’s materialism and selfishness influenced by the way she pursues men to be more powerful, dominant and important in society? Is she like this because she wants to feel like she is different, not necessarily special, from the other women? Or is that the only she sees fit in order to feel like she has some control over her own life in this man-dominated society? Or is she trying to shy away from the typical gendered roles of a women, such as being a devout wife and a good mother?
-Does Emma reject her role as a woman? We read on page 1089 that Emma desires to give birth to a son rather than a daughter beacuase men are "free to range the passions and the world, to surmount obstacles, to taste the rarest pleasures" whereas women are "continually thwarted." In addition, we often observe Emma in boredom from the daily task of a woman. However, she loves fashion, doesn't want to work, and definitely pays the role of a naive female in her relationship with Rodolphe. Does Emma truly reject her role?
-Why did Flaubert decide to make Emma the more manipulative one in the relationship rather than Charles, although the man was supposed to play the more manipulative/ dominant role in marriages in society back then. Is it because Flabuert is a man and wants to make the male figure look better than the female?
-How do Emma's actions demonstrate her taste in men? Do you think Charles will ever fit in her "ideal man" category? Why?
-Emma is disappointed when she gives birth to a girl, because she knows she lives in a man's world and life is easier for men. In what ways is her behavior toward men (particularly Chrales, Léon, Rodolphe) a means to overcome the difficulties/barriers faced by women?
-How does Flaubert show his view on women in Madame Bovary espeicaly in the sense of their relationship with men? How do the women's actions and emotions reflect his views on women?
-Why do you think Flaubert had Emma be a cheater with so many other men? Do you think it has to do with Flaubert being a man?
-In the novel, Emma is bored with life, does not have a solid job or any type of career and cannot make up her mind who she is in love with. Why does Flaubert make women seem so useless, unfaithful, and indecisive? Does Emma like her role in life or does she want more out of her life?
-How does Emma reveal contemporary gender roles when she talks about why she would prefer to have a boy over a girl?
-In Madame Bovary, there is a scene where the sex of the baby is found out. It was a girl. Emma did not seem to be happy that she had bourne a girl. Why was this? Is this because of the time period in which the story takes place in which boys were more valued than girls? Does this reaction reflect on Emma or Charles in any way?
-How are women's lives portrayed in Madame Bovary?
-How does Flaubert feel about women and how does he bring his opinion into the story?
-Does Flaubert give women masculine qualities?
-Why does Flaubert choose to make women the main interest of his story? A woman is even the title of his story.
-Are women powerful or powerless in Flaubert's story?
-How are Emma's love affairs a metaphor of her mood and financial state?
-Emma says "A woman is always thwarted" from experiencing the world, feeling true passions and tasting the rarest pleasures. (1089) How do her negative feelings toward her own sex influence her actions and relationships in the private and public spheres?
-What makes the qualities portrayed in Emma, Félicité and the elder Bovary (and other female characters) different form the qualities portrayed in Charles, Léon and Homais (and other male characters)? Do these characteristics hold true for the 19th century?
-How does Gustave Flaubert's opinion of women's role in society come out in the story? Does he agree with the normal idea of a woman's role in his time?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM
-Was it a custom in 19th century France for parents to choose who their children married, as Charles's mother and father did for him when they selected his first wife?
-If Flaubert actually wrote Madame Bovary in the romantic period rather than the Realistic period, how different would it be?
-How does the work reflect the lives of people and their values in the period in which it was written? Are characters like Madame Bovary and Homais the reflection of typical human beings found in this historical period?
-How does each character reflect different attributes of the nineteenth century realism? How does Emma represent a romantic character?
-Romantic writers typically rejected rationalism and would portray this through their writing. How does Flaubert's rejction of rationalism show through Charles and Emma in Madame Bovary?
-How does Charles' approach to dealing with the loss of those close to him reflect the late 19th century?
-Does Emma's affair with Leon have a significant purpose during this time period?
-During the 1850s in France we saw a rise in the class known as the "haute bourgeoisie." In what descriptions and with what characters can we see Flaubert's disgust towards this class of people?
-Madame Bovary is considered the showpiece of French realism. How does Madame Bovary represent French realism and its time period (mid-1800s)?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-Did Flaubert have a negligent mother, like Berthe did? Does any of his upbringing pay a part in Madame Bovary?
-Have the psychological mindsets and personality traits of these characters played a major role in the outcome of their individual lives, that is, where they are at the end of the novel?
-How does the emotion of lust affect the overall personality and psychological health of Emma? How does the constant failutre of achieving total sexual (ie finding a happy relationship) satisfaction and materialistic satisfaction change her personality throughout the story?
-As you know, Madame Bovary was published in France in 1857. Do you think any of the characters represent the psycholgy of certain social groups during this time period?
-How does the character's work affect his/her behavior or thought process? What does it reveal about their mood? Do you think the character's work/duty has an impact on how they present themselves in society and how they view their placement in the world?
-Do you think a character's emotions and behavior define who they are? Are they expressed internally or externally and are the characters able to keep them under control or do they let their emotions consume them?
-What does it say about a character's psychological state when the author switches to their inner thoughts (free indirect discourse) without warning the reader? What does it say about a character's desires, dreams or fantasies?
-How would you describe the psychological state or states of Emma? What types of events or emotions does she experience to alter her psychological state?
-[How] Do you think Rodophe's confidence and his dignity come in the way on a quest of finding true love?
-Are the main characters "forced" to love because of emotional setbacks? Why or why not?
-Do Emma and Lheureux manifest a smiliar (or even the same) psychological personality? The two act in a quite reprehensible manner; could such activity indicate a similarity? (Essentially, can Emma and Lheureux give together an example of a reprehensible psychology?)
-Does the author present psychological matters such as repression, dreams, and desire, either consciously or unconsciously?
-How do the characters of Madame Bovary, Charles and Emma, reflect Flaubert's own psychological state of mind?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM -
-In what way does the author clue in the reader the motives and intentions of certain characters before they are revealed to the other characters in the book and what kind of emotions does it invoke?
-With his use of descriptive, imagery-filled vocabulary, Gustave Flaubert has the tendency to "ramble" on about certain people, situations, animals, etc. Describe the importance of any one person, situation, and animal that has significant meaning to you (cultural, religious, physical similarities).

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-How is the use of nature in Madame Bovary evident in its imagery, mood, language and theme?
-How does the town's excitement about the agricultural fair show the relationship people in Yonville at the time have with the environment?

• QUEER THEORY
- Every new male character that is introduced to Emma Bovary seems to be automatically attracted to her. Do you believe that Charles really has no idea that Emma is flirting with guys behind his back? What role does sexuality play amongst the characters of Madame Bovary? Is physical beauty the only reason why all the male characters seem to be attracted to Emma? Do you think Emma is a promiscuous girl?

2) Also: "The Oedipus complex in Freudian psychoanalysis refers to stage of psychosexual development in childhood where children of both sexes regard their father as an adversary and competitor for the exclusive love of their mother. The name of the complex derives from the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unwittingly kills his father Laius and marries his mother Jocasta.

Freud considered the successful resolution of the Oedipus complex to be key to the development of gender roles and identity, with boys and girls resolving the conflicts differently as a result of castration anxiety and penis envy. Freud also held that the unsuccessful resolution of the Oedipus complex could result in neurosis and homosexuality.… Although common usage refers to 'suffering from an Oedipus complex', psychoanalysis does not consider the complex a pathology, but instead a perfectly normal stage that all children go through. Oedipal desires are thought to remain heavily repressed and unconsciously in the minds of all functioning adults."

Choose one of Shannon's 6 types of critical questions (Formalist, Sociological: Marxist, Feminist, Cultural and Historical, Psychological, or Reader-Response, pages 61-4 + Queer and Ecological Criticism). Write a question, then answer it, based on the first half of The Metamorphosis. Be sure to tell me what category you have chosen. See assignment for Class 16 above for some examples of questions inspired by Madame Bovary. Remember Atwood's quote: The answers you get from literature depend on the questions you pose.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM
-What are some of the issues raised in how the family treats Gregory after he transformed into a monstrous vermin? Are these issues resolved?
-How do the characters' physical struggles and pains go along with what is happening to them internally?
-Kafka once wrote "The dream reveals the reality which conception lags behind, that is the horror of life, the terror of art." How is Gregor's metamorphosis into an insect representative of this quote?
-How does Gregory's thoughts towards life and his family change over time? How does his family's view of him change over time?
-Does the author use Gregory's transformation into an insect as a metaphor for something else?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
Marxist Criticism:
-Does Gregor's lack of income mean that it is his fault his family is struggling?
-How are class differences presented in this work? Are the characters aware or unaware of the economic and social forces that affect their lives?
-How does their economic condition affect not only Gregory's life, but his family's lives as well?
-Is this sudden change and metamorphosis a result of the social and economic condition of Gregor’s family and his surroundings?
-How does social class and economic standing affect the characters?
-Is the fact that Gregory turns into a bug an indication of his social and economic status?
-How do economic conditions determine the Samsas' lives?

Feminist Criticism:
-How are women's lives portrayed in the work? Do women accept or reject these roles?
-How does Gregor's sister Grete's actions and role, such as taking charge of Gregor's situation, demonstrate a change in the views of women's roles or duties in this age of modernist writing?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM
-How does The Metamorphosis reflect the Expressionist period in which it was created?
-How is this story representative of the Modernist period and how does it differ from the other works we have read from this period?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-By all accounts, Gregor is seemingly a good, moral and caring person. He works off his parents' debt, has never taken a sick day, and is generally good to his family. Why did this metamorphosis happen and why does he seem so OK with it?
-How does The Metamorphosis reflect Kafka's personal psychology? His own life and problems?
-Can parents grow to accept and love a son that has transformed into a socially unaccpetable "person"? At what point will the emotions subside and acceptance occur? Can this transformation happen at all?
-What do different characters' behaviors towards Greogory after he transformed say about their emotional state?
-What does Gregor's emotional and behavior reveal about his psychological state?
-Does Kafka present psychological matters such as repression, dreams, and desire, either consciously or unconsciously?
-How does Gregor Sama's behavior change when he finds out he is alienated?
-Does Kafka identify with any of the characters in the story The Metamorphosis?
-Why is it that Gregory's family begins to not care about him once he becomes a bug?
-How does Gregor relate himself to his social situation?
-Kafka's Metamorphosis is unique in that it combines realism with the nightmarish as Gregory Samsa has changed into a giant bug. How did you respond to Kafka's writing style in relation to enjoyment and intepretation of the story?
- How do you think Gregor feels when his parents neglect him although he provides for them financially?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM
-Even though it is impossible for a human to be magically transformed into an insect, how can the reader relate to Gregor Samsa?
-How does Kafka convey the gruesome detail of Greogroy's weird condition throughout the story?
-Have you ever felt like a bug, as in very different from anyone else?
-The reader does not know all of Gregory's most inner thoughts. Describe how YOU think he is feeling on the inside (sad, scared, guilty, confused, etc). When all is said and done, do you think he desires to be changed back into a human? Is he hopeful and/or confident in his metamorphosis back to a human?
-Discuss the positives and negatives of being transformed into an insect. If you were to undergo such a transformation, what would you want to become?
-In your opinion, do you think that Gregory actually turned into an insect or did HE believe he was changed into an insect? Was it a result of imagination, dream, or insanity?
-Thus far, what characters undergo changes (is Gregory the only one)? Explain with examples.

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM
-How does Gregor's job prioritize his emotions?
-How do the environmental conditions in the introductory part of the story affect the mood of the story?
-Why does Kafka change Gregory into an insect out of all the creatures on the planet?

• QUEER THEORY
After Gregor experiences his change into a large insect, he is no longer able to work, leave his room or do much of anything for himself. What impact does this metamorphosis have on gender roles and roles of authority within the Samsa household?
Why did Kafka choose to establish Gregory's lack of romantic relationships first and why is that significant in his isolation?
