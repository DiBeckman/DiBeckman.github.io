COURSE DESCRIPTION
In this course we will explore the Western literary tradition through reading, discussing, and writing about some of the major works of European literature from the eighteenth through twentieth centuries. We will also pay attention to the political and cultural contexts of these works. Students will "engage the human experience through the intepretation of human culture [and] become aware of the act of interpretation itself as a critical form of knowing in the humanities." (General Education Program, NCSU)

LEARNING GOALS
By the end of the course you should be able to:
• explain the characteristics of the major literary periods of Western Culture from the Enlightenment to Modernism, comparing "ideas, values, images, cultural artifacts, economic structures, technological developments, [and] attitudes." (General Education Program, NCSU)
• describe how a literary text reflects its historical context
• identify the literary and rhetorical devices employed in a text, such as genre, point of view, irony, etc., and relate them to the content of the text (See Course Glossary)
• discuss, orally and in writing, selected literary texts of Western Culture from the 18th century to the first half of the 20th century and their continuing aesthetic validity or challenge
• write a coherent essay on given topics about these texts and their contexts, supporting arguments with evidence (quotes) from the literary texts. The learning objective is for students to "make academic arguments about the human experience using reasons and evidence for supporting those reasons that are appropriate to the humanities." (General Education Program, NCSU).

In addition to a first introductory post, you will be required to post at least 7 original contributions (abbreviated as oc on the syllabus) on 7 different authors, each one at least 7 lines long when posted. You should make at least one good point and provide evidence for your statement, for example, explain your interpretation or support it with a quote from the text. You can also make comparisons with other texts we have studied, make a comment on a general theme (social rules and restrictions, utopia, boundaries), a specific use of language, or invite discussion on a debatable point. In addition, you are required to post 6 responses to contributions by others, on 6 authors. Each one must be at least 5 lines long. Here, too, please support your comment with evidence. Before you post, please read what has already been written to avoid duplication and a failing grade for that post. For the responses, please comment directly on what has been written by at least one other student. It is fine to post an original contribution and response on the same author.

Dates by which your introduction, 7 original contributions and 6 responses should be posted are listed on the Syllabus. Since there are more than 17 authors you have flexibility; however, late postings will not count towards your grade. Cutoff is midnight when the date changes. You are responsible for a total of 14 posts, including your self-introductory post to the class.

Discussion Board postings will be graded pass/fail. If you pass, you'll get full credit: 10% of your final grade. If your first post is graded a fail, we will discuss in the office and you'll have another chance. In order not to get behind on your postings, I recommend that you make at least 3 original contributions and 3 responses by midterm. You may post as often as you like to contribute to the discussion, as long as you remain on topic.

READING NOTEBOOK
As an alternative to the Discussion Board or for extra credit (see below), you may choose to keep a Reading Notebook which will be turned in 4 times over the semester as indicated on the syllabus. I recommend using a ring binder with tab dividers, but you can organize it your own way as long as you include the information listed below. For example, some students prefer to organize their notebooks chronologically, with Just the Facts, Vocubulary and Lightning for each text all together. Others prefer to keep each section separate. You will be graded on completeness, thoughtfulness, and creativity: see Notebook Grading Rubric. Print out a copy of the rubric to sign and submit with your Notebook.

Section 1: Just the facts. One page for each work we study. Include title, date, author, original language, main characters and setting, and a one paragraph plot summary. These pages will be extremely helpful when you study for exams.

Section 2: Vocabulary. List of new or unfamiliar words you encounter while reading or hear in class, including literary terms, along with their definitions. Use an online or paper dictionary to look up words you don't know, such as http://www.wordreference.com/ (See also Shannon’s Glossary of Literary Terms, pp. 202-208.) You should have entries for each text.

Section 3: Lightning: what struck you while you were reading.
For example: copy down memorable or provocative passages or images; include your personal response to something you’ve read--what it made you think of in your own life.

Shannon’s 4 questions (p. 27) are also helpful guides:
What is confusing?
What is repeated?
What is familiar?
What is strange?

Here is an example from a student's homework: "However a strange phrase with I was not familiar was the image of a rose dying in aromatic pain. To say this expression took my breath away would be cliché, but it just about did. The way Alexander Pope wrote this phrase made me feel as if I had been shocked by an electric current. The rose, while doing its job, shows no sign of anguish whatsoever. This flower presents itself in a content fashion, not questioning its way of life. It may suggest to us humans to follow its example and go along with the positions to which God has designated us."

Another idea is to use the web to search for quotes by a particular author and find one that sparks some thoughts in your mind. I typically use Google and type in the author's name and the word quotations.

On the grading rubric, you'll notice that I write: After the first submission, response to instructor feedback is included. This means that I expect you to write responses to the some of the comments I write on your notebook pages or homework. The Reading Notebook, like the daily writing assignments, is ideally a dialogue between teacher and student.

These notes and comments will be helpful when you participate in class discussion and are planning your papers and taking exams.

Section 4: Optional. Class reflections, notes, written work and tests returned, etc.

EXAMS AND QUIZZES: Students prepare assigned reading (see Syllabus) before coming to class. There may be a pop-quiz during the semester.

There will be a midterm and a final exam. Make-up for exams will be granted only if you are excused in advance with proper documentation.

Exams will consist of:
1) Identification of selected passages. Identify the author, work, approximate date, and original language. Briefly discuss the passage and its relevance to the work as a whole with attention to both form and content.
2) Short essay (approx. 300 words) on the literary movements studied. You will be expected to define and illustrated the movement(s) by referring to authors, works, main ideas, relevant literary terms (satire, autobiography, absurd) and any appropriate social or historical events.
3) Long essay (400-500 words) on a given topic or theme and how it is treated in at least two works we have studied.

PAPERS
In addition to the informal writing assignments, you will submit two papers in draft and final form: the first should be 4-6 pages and the second 5-8 pages. Due dates are listed on the Syllabus and assignment descriptions given in Paper 1 and Paper 2.

The honor pledge, “I have neither given nor received unauthorized assistance on this assignment” is included on the Self-evaluation and Reflective Statements which you will turn in with your final draft. You are responsible for printing and filling out the appropriate form for each paper: Self-Evaluation and Reflective Statement for Paper 1, Self-Evaluation and Reflective Statement for Paper 2 or Self-Evaluation and Reflection Statement for Creative Rewriting Papers.

We will work with Edward A. Shannon’s book to support your writing about literature. You do not need to consult critical literature on the author or the text, but should engage yourself closely with the text, or an aspect of it, and formulate your thoughts about it in a organized way. I am most interested in your own original ideas, not someone else’s, nor in a paraphrase of my comments from class, our class-discussions, or the readings themselves.

If you use ideas or quotes from critical literature (books, articles, internet, etc.), you need to document your sources and give proper credit to someone else’s ideas whether you quote directly or rephrase their ideas. Otherwise you violate academic integrity and commit plagiarism (see below for policy). Do not make someone else’s ideas the major focus of your paper. For the format of footnotes and bibliography, if you have them, refer to the MLA Style Manual (available in bookstore or library) or Chapter 6 in Shannon. You can also use the NCSU Libraries' Citation Builder.

Papers are due at the beginning of the class period. Email submissions and late submissions will only be accepted with my permission.

See Poetry Presentation, Paper 1 and Paper 2 for details on each assignment.

See The Scoring Rubric to see how your paper will be graded and what to strive for in the areas of content, organization and format, grammar, vocabulary and fluency, and supporting documents.

Supporting documents: We will use a peer review process as well as instructor review to help you improve your writing. See the Peer Review Feedback form. Print out and bring two copies of this form for your classmates to fill out for you. You will also fill out a Self-Evaluation form to include along with the Peer Review Feedback forms and first drafts when you turn in your final draft. You will lose points on your paper if these documents are not included. See Scoring Rubric for details.

Help with your writing assignments and papers is also available at Writing and Speaking Tutorial Services. Visit their web site at http://www.ncsu.edu/tutorial_center/writespeak/index.html

HONOR CODE: The Foreign Language Department takes the University Policy on Academic Integrity very seriously. An atmosphere of trust, honesty and respect for each other fosters educational progress and success. Please do not give me any reason to be suspicious of your honesty. I ask each student to fill out and return the FL 222 Academic Integrity Form. Please read it carefully before signing.

You are to do your own work on all class assignments, papers, and exams, unless it is a designated group assignment. You must not incorporate others’ ideas or words and present them as your own—this is called plagiarism. Whether you take ideas (verbatim or just as an idea) from books, the internet, or other sources does not matter; you need to acknowledge their source if they are not your own. Let me warn you especially about the temptations of the internet. You will find more details in Shannon, pp. 148-150.

The NC State Library has a Plagiarism Tutorial at: http://www.lib.ncsu.edu/scc/tutorial/plagiarism/
Please ask me about anything that remains unclear.

Violations of academic integrity (plagiarism, cheating, etc.) will be prosecuted according to NC State’s policy on academic integrity, posted at: http://www.ncsu.edu/policies/student_services/student_discipline/POL11.35.1.php

DIVERSITY POLICY:
This course fosters free and open dialogue, the acceptance and discussion of different opinions, and mutual respect among class members. Please consult NCSU Policies on Non-Discrimination .

Every member of the university community has the right to work and learn in an environment free from discrimination and harassment. Since everyone has this right, everyone also has the responsibility to help prevent discrimination or harassment from occurring. There are some simple ways to prevent discrimination and harassment. One of the most important ways is to respect others and make sure your actions serve as a role model for others. You can also:
• Get to know those around you.
• Do not talk about topics such as sex, race, ethnicity, or religion on the job or in the classroom unless it is germane to the discussion.
• Refrain from using jokes, words, and gestures with sexual, racial, religious, or other discriminatory connotations.
• Respect others' personal space.
• Avoid making assumptions about others.
