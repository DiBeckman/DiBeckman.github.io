5. identify aspects of the cultures of the French-speaking world.

WELCOME TO FRENCH! This course aims at giving you a solid foundation in understanding, speaking, reading and writing the French language at the elementary level. To accomplish these goals, it is essential that we use French as the language of the classroom. Handy phrases for communicating in French:

Répétez, s'il vous plaît. = Repeat, please.
Une minute, s'il vous plaît.= Wait a minute, please.
Comment dit-on [English word] en français? = How do you say [English word] in French.?
Que veut dire [French word]?= What does [French word] mean ?
Qu'est-ce que c'est? = What's that?
Je ne sais pas. = I don't know.
Je ne comprends pas. = I don't understand.

Although the language of instruction and interaction in my classroom is French, I do not expect you to understand every single word I am saying. Do not hesitate to let me know if your confusion gets in the way of class participation. I will find another way of getting my point across, whether it is by repeating, paraphrasing, elaborating, or simply slowing down. Raising your hand in class is the best way to let me know you have a question. Alternatively, you are welcome to see me after class or during my office hours. Every student will benefit from one-to-one time with me whether you have questions about grammar, pronunciation, or simply to practice speaking French.

HOW TO DO WELL IN CLASS:

1) Prepare assignments before coming to class so we can get the most out of our class time together. Assignments are to be completed as indicated on the syllabus. I expect you to have studied the grammar points as explained in the textbook and completed the homework before coming to class. In class, I will be presenting the same material in French as a review before we do face-to-face exercises (teacher-student and student-student) in class. Some students find it helpful to read written explanations in both Motifs and English Grammar for Students of French before coming to class, for difficult grammar points. You will find the approach that works best for you, depending on your learning style.

2) Homework is essential. There is a significant amount of work for this class. It is important to keep up and impossible to cram for this course and do well. You will not be able to absorb all the grammar points from classroom instruction alone. That is why there is homework, a lab manual, resources on the Cengage site, and my Supplementary Materials page. Learning a language is like building a house of cards: each one must be well in place before adding the next. Getting behind can lead to serious difficulties. Spend at least 30 minutes on French per day. Regular repetition of small quantities of information is the most successful method for learning a foreign language.

3) Participate actively in class. Mentally answer each and every question when a classmate is responding. Class participation and homework make up 25% of your grade. The more you participate, the better your grade will be, and the better your French will become. Your class grade reflects your performance in class (attendance, knowledge of daily lesson, volunteering answers, asking questions). Take listening comprehension seriously — it will help both your proficiency and your grade. There will be a listening component on all exams.

4) Take risks. Speak out. Don’t be afraid to make mistakes. I am here to help. The more you speak, the more you will get out of the class. Save perfection for exams. The desire to communicate is the most important consideration when speaking.

5) Speak French as much as possible in the classroom. Speak French outside the classroom whenever possible, too. Listen to French music, go to French movies, eat French food!

6) Do not chew gum in class. Having something in your mouth makes it harder to pronounce French correctly.

7) Bring Motifs to class every day. This is essential. Bring a portable French/English-English/French dictionary to class, if you find it useful. Alternatively, you can consult an online resource such as http://wordreference.com or ask me for vocabulary assistance in class.

8) Take notes in class whenever appropriate. Buy 3x5 notecards to make personalized verb charts and other study aids. I like to use colored index cards with pink for feminine nouns and blue for masculine. Study with a friend outside of class. There are excellent study tips in English Grammar for Students of French.

9) Cell phones must be turned off and mobile devices out of sight during class (unless you are using a dictionary app during for class work). If you need to know what time it is, you may ask me (in French, of course: Quelle heure est-il?)

I will do my best to keep the class atmosphere creative, lively, and accepting of your best efforts. Both effort and ability will be rewarded. In return, you, too have some responsibilities. Please read carefully and follow the advice above and the information given below.

HOMEWORK: Each module of the textbook is divided into two parts: 1) White Pages (Thèmes, Pratiques de conversation, Perspectives culturelles, Écoutons ensemble, Lecture, Expression écrite) and 2) Green Pages (Structures utiles)

Before each class, look through the white pages (column 2 of the syllabus) so you know what we will be doing in class. The audio for each Module (indicated by the speaker graphic) is included on the Cengage web site. Listening to the exercises and the vocabulary will help you get used to hearing French.

The third column in the syllabus lists selected Lecture: reading exercises. There are corresponding written exercises to turn in listed on the syllabus (Compréhension et intégration; Et vous? Avez-vous compris?) I will correct these for you. If you are having trouble with the reading questions, try writing out the question and translating it into English. I'll correct that, too. If you are struggle with a reading, we can go over it together during an office hour.

Your main homework is to write out the answers to exercises in the green pages (column 4 of the syllabus). You may choose to do the even or odd questions for each exercise, except for Tout Ensemble, where it makes sense to do all. Check this work using Appendix B (page 491 ff. in the textbook). All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. If there are no errors, put a check mark by each section so I know that you've corrected your work. If you have questions about an exercise, write me a note on your homework. Reminder: copying answers from the textbook or workbook answer key without trying them on your own first, is not only a waste of time (what would you learn by copying?), but also an Academic Integrity violation (see above). If you have no idea what the exercise is about, it makes sense to do one or two questions and check the answers to see what is expected. You may also contact me. Try your best. You will never be graded on the number of errors in your homework or workbook. Mistakes are normal when you are learning, and I expect to see mistakes and corrections on all of your homework. Revising and correcting your own errors is the best way to ensure that you do not make the same error on a test.

You will turn in the corrected work from the green pages daily and all other written assignments when indicated on the syllabus. Please put the page numbers at the top of each homework assignment. Full credit will be given only for corrected work turned in on time.

You will also do the exercises in the Workbook/Lab Manual pages corresponding to each Module. All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. The answers are in the Workbook Answer Key. For copyright reasons, I have placed a copy of the key on reserve in DH Hill Library and posted it on our Moodle site: If there are no errors, put a check mark by each exercise so I know at a glance that you've corrected your work. Mark any sections you don't understand so that you can ask about them in class or I can explain when I'm correcting your work.

In the first half of the Workbook, you may do even or odd questions in each exercise, but you must do each exercise. Except for Module 5, you will skip Synthèse, as indicated on the Syllabus. In the second half of the Workbook/Lab manual, you will need to do all the questions. Suggested completion dates are indicated on the syllabus. Bring your Workbook/Lab Manual exercises to each exam so that I can give you credit for your work. You may write in your book or on a separate sheet of paper. You will get full credit (10 points) for complete and corrected (in a different color) exercises and partial credit (5 points) for uncorrected exercises or late work.

Listening Comprehension and Pronunciation Exercises in the Lab Manual (final section of the textbook) are to be completed with the audio available online, either on the Cengage site or our Moodle site.

In additional to the daily homework, you will also read and complete the self-correcting exercises in English Grammar for Students of French. Assigned sections for each exam are listed on the Syllabus. If you have the 5th edition, exercises are at the end of each chapter and corrections are in the back of the book. If you have the 6th edition, use the French_review_Booklet.pdf to find both exercises and the answer key. If you are interested, a very detailed listing of the sections which correlate to the grammar we are studying in Motifs is listed in the Correlation Guide pdf. The completed and corrected exercises are due each exam day for full credit (5 points).

COMPOSITIONS Expression écrite and Synthèse.
You will write a double-spaced, handwritten or word-processed composition for each Module, submitting a first draft and final version for each. The due date for the first draft is indicated on the syllabus. Learn to type accents on your computer. You will find an explanation of how to do this on the Supplementary Materials page (see Accents). Drafts will be returned with corrections indicated. Beginning with Module 3, I will be giving you a grade for the revised compositions. Unrevised compositions will receive a check mark which corresponds to a C when I calculate the final grade. Compositions will be graded using the Writing Assessment Rubric.

Reminder: do not use on-line translators for writing assignments for more than individual words or phrases. Do use your dictionary or http://www.wordreference.com/.

When you use a dictionary, make sure that your word fits the context in French. For example: the noun match in English may correspond to un match in French (a sporting event) or une allumette (used to light a fire). Context matters. For more information about how to use a bilingual foreign language dictionary, see the link to the Dictionary Skills Resource Pack in the previous section.
