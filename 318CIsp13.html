COURSE DESCRIPTION

In this course we will explore the heritage of the French cinema through watching, reading, discussing, and writing about some of the major films from the nineteenth through twenty-first centuries. We will also pay attention to the political and cultural contexts of these works. Students will "engage the human experience through the interpretation of human culture [and] become aware of the act of interpretation itself as a critical form of knowing in the humanities." (General Education Program, NCSU)

LEARNING GOALS

By the end of the course you should be able to:

  • explain the characteristics of the major periods of French cinema from its invention to the present day comparing "ideas, values, images, cultural artifacts, economic structures, technological developments, [and] attitudes." (General Education Program, NCSU)
  • describe how a film reflects its historical context.
  • identify the narrative and cinematic devices employed in a film, such as theme, point of view, mise-en scène, and composition, and relate them to the content of the film. See Glossaries in Apprentissage (3-7) and A Short Guide (174-177) for definitions of these terms.
  • discuss, orally and in writing, selected French films and their continuing aesthetic validity or challenge.
  • write a coherent essay on given topics about these films and their contexts, supporting arguments with evidence. The learning objective is for students to "make academic arguments about the human experience using reasons and evidence for supporting those reasons that are appropriate to the humanities." (General Education Program, NCSU).

HOMEWORK

Students view and write about assigned films (see Syllabus) before coming to class. The informal daily writing assignments are posted on the Les Devoirs page. They do not appear on the Syllabus. You may type or handwrite your daily writing assignment. Double space or skip lines, so that I can read them easily and there will be room for my comments. Please include an assignment heading (for example, Fantômas, La Passion de Jeanne d'Arc). You may also wish to copy and paste the prompt as a heading if you are typing the assignment. If you use a spiral notebook, please remove the little bits of paper on the left-hand edge before turning in your homework. Feel free to use recycled paper. I appreciate legibility and ecological efforts!

In your daily writing assignments, as on longer papers, be sure to cite your secondary sources. Not citing them can lead to a failing grade on the assignment or in the course. See the section on the Academic Integrity below.

WRITTEN WORK

Please skip lines on all compositions and longer written assignments whether handwritten or typed.

Do use regular or online dictionaries to check gender, spelling, and find French or English equivalents for individual words or idiomatic expressions (for example: tourner un film = to make a film). Bookmark these sites:

  • http://www.wordreference.com/ (my favorite; great for idioms)
  • oxfordlanguagedictionaries (Sign-on via NCSU Libraries. Includes useful links and resources including the Dictionary Skills Resource Pack)

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY

The Foreign Language Department takes the University Policy on Academic Integrity very seriously. An atmosphere of trust, honesty and respect fosters educational progress and success. Please do not give me any reason to be suspicious of your honesty. I ask each student to fill out and return the FLF 318 Academic Integrity Form. Please read it carefully before signing.

You are expected to do your own work on all class assignments, papers, and exams, unless it is a designated group assignment. You must not incorporate others’ ideas or words and present them as your own—this is called plagiarism. Whether you take ideas (verbatim or just as an idea) from books, the internet, or other sources does not matter; you need to acknowledge their source if they are not your own. Let me warn you especially about the temptations of the internet. There are appropriate and inappropriate uses of sites such as Wikipedia in this class. Not citing your source is always inappropriate, and may result in a failing grade on the assignment or for the course.

Violations of academic integrity (plagiarism, cheating, etc.) will be prosecuted according to NC State’s Code of Student Conduct.

DISCUSSION BOARD: Moodle

To enhance your involvement with the films and extend class participation outside of our limited class time, you will be sharing observations and opinions on a class discussion board. To access Moodle, go to http://moodle.wolfware.ncsu.edu and log in with your Unity ID and password. Once logged in, you will be brought to your my Courses page. Beneath the Courses header you will find links to the home pages of classes in which you are enrolled that are using Moodle. Click on FLF 318. This will take you to the FLF 318 home page.

If this is your first visit to Moodle, you may wish to consult Moodle Student Orientation under Learning with Moodle: A Student's Guide to Using Moodle.

On the FLF 318 page, you will also find the Discussion des films Topics block where the topics are listed.

For help with Moodle, follow the Help links or call 515-HELP.

You will be required to post at least 8 original contributions on 8 different films, each one at least 5 sentences long. You can use Les Questions or Corrigan's Screening Report (8-9) to get ideas. You should make at least one good point and provide evidence for your statement. Explain your observation, interpretation and support it with specific details. For example, you can make comparisons with other films you have seen or we have studied in class or make a comment on a general theme or a specific use of cinematic technique. Following up your personal observation with a question is a good strategy. The best posts are thought-provoking for the rest of the class.

The Moodle Forum is not Rotten Tomatoes. Your reviews should go beyond simply what you like and don't like. The purpose of the class is to get you out of your comfort zone and learn to observe and appreciate why the filmmakers chose to tell the stories they did and what the films reflect about French history and culture.

In addition, you are required to post 7 responses to contributions by others, on 7 different films. Each response post must be at least 5 sentences long. Here, too, please support your comment with evidence. Before you post, please read what has already been written to avoid duplication and a failing grade for that post. In your response, please comment directly on what has been written by another student, mentioning him/her by name or quoting his/her post. It is fine to post an original contribution and response to some one else's post on the same film. Each of these posts will earn you a point towards your final grade.

Posts will be due at midnight on the Sunday following class discussion of the film. Responses to those original contribution posts are due the following Sunday at midnight. You can check the number of posts you have made on the Forum Posts or Activity Reports tabs on your Participant page in Moodle.

If you submit 15 acceptable posts, you'll get full credit: 15% of your final grade. If your first post is unacceptable, I will let you know by email, and you'll have another chance. If your posts are too short, you will only receive partial credit and if they are late, you won't receive any credit. In order not to get behind on your postings, I recommend that you make at least 3 original contributions and 3 responses by midterm. You may post as often as you like to contribute to the discussion, as long as you remain on topic.

There is a list of American and French Supplementary Films in the right-hand column of the Syllabus that I believe are valuable additions to the required films. I have listed them in connection with the time period being studied that week. On the Moodle page, they are listed with (fs=film supplémentaire) after their title. Beyond those, my husband and I have also made short films in France every summer as part of Summer Study Abroad in Lille. Comment on any of those films under the Les Films de Lille sur vimeo heading. You may post on any of these supplementary films at any time during the semester. These posts will be worth 1/2 point each.

La Structure de la Présentation

  • De quel genre de film s'agit-il?
  • Résumez l'intrigue du film. Expliquez la structure et les thèmes du film et notez le vocabulaire essentiel ou spécifique au film.
  • Décrivez les personnages principaux.
  • Choisissez une scène et commentez cette scène et sa technique cinématographique.
  • Comment est-ce que ce film fait partie de l'héritage du cinéma français?
  • Qu'est-ce que vous avez apprécié le plus dans le film?

Préparez une fiche pédagogique pour le professeur avec le titre du film, le réalisateur, les personnages et acteurs principaux, notes sur le vocabulaire, la structure, le thèmes, et les sources consultées.

For your final project you may choose to write a paper in French (6-9 pages) or make your own film in French (5 minutes). You may also choose to work with a partner for the paper or with a group for the film. For your paper, you may choose to write about the film you present in class or another French film. I am flexible about the precise topic: please choose something that you find compelling. The paper can follow the model of the chapters on each film in Singerman (without the dossier critique), the model of Corrigan's "Critical Essay" (12-14; with sample essays 78-81; 96-102; 150-154), or focus on a particular actor, director, or other film-related topic. The peer review draft of your term project is due April 8 and the complete rough draft due on the day of your presentation, April 17-24. The final project will be due Monday, April 29, University Reading Day in the main FLL Office, Withers 310. Late submissions will only be accepted with my permission, and must be arranged before the due date of the paper. Otherwise, you will lose 10 points each day that the assignment is late.

See the Scoring Rubric (for the term project) to see how your papers will be graded and what to strive for in the areas of content, organization and format, grammar, vocabulary and fluency. A Short Guide to Writing About Film Chapters 5-7 includes details about what is expected for style, research and manuscipt form.

DIVERSITY POLICY

This course fosters free and open dialogue, the acceptance and discussion of different opinions, and mutual respect among class members. Since class participation is required, it is important that students not be afraid to speak up and share their ideas. If you ever feel that you have been intimidated or treated disrespectfully in class, by me or by another student, please come see me in private as soon as possible. That is not the environment I desire for this class.

Every member of the university community has the right to work and learn in an environment free from discrimination and harassment. Since everyone has this right, everyone also has the responsibility to help prevent discrimination or harassment from occurring. One of the most important ways is to respect others and make sure your actions serve as a role model for others. You can also:

  • Get to know those around you.
  • Our literary texts discuss sensitive topics such as sex, race, ethnicity, and religion. Your observations on these topics should relate to the works we are studying and your responses to them.
  • Refrain from using jokes, words, and gestures with sexual, racial, religious, or other discriminatory connotations.
  • Respect others' personal space.
  • Avoid making assumptions about others. Ask for their opinions and insights.
  • As members of a university community, we all have the right to work and learn without being discriminated against or harassed. We also have some responsibilities — to help prevent discrimination and harassment, and to help address it when it does occur.
