Here are two additional, more modern, sets of critical questions you may choose to address. They were written by a former student, Garry Pigg (Fall, 2005)

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM

1. How do the environmental/ecological conditions within the setting of the work affect the language, mood, tone, and theme?
2. How does the use of the word "nature" in the work reflect the current scientific beliefs of the time and how does that affect the characters, plot, themes?
3. How does the relationship between man/woman and his/her environment--economically, agriculturally, botanically, socially--affect the characters, language, theme, plot, and tone? i.e. do the characters have to hunt and gather, farm, trade, live in city, country?
4. How do plants, animals, and their conditions in the environment affect the character's livelihood, the plot, the author's tone, the moods, the theme? i.e. the effects of individual species such as cows, camels, horses,goats, bananas, sugar, cotton, corn, potatoes, fungi, bacteria,
viruses; and ambient conditions such as drought, flooding, pollution, climate change, introduction of invasive species, and famine.

QUEER THEORY

1. How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plot, and themes?
2. How does sexual identity affect the characters? How do they deal with others and their own sexual deviance or breaking of social norms?
3. How does homosexuality or bisexuality play a role in the characters' lives, the tone, mood, theme, and plot of the work?
4. What role does sexuality play in the work? How does it affect the use of language? Does it represent love and beauty or marriage and
reproduction?
5. How does religion affect the tendencies of the characters to express or not express themselves sexually?
6. How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sexuality to define "others"? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism? Does it hurt or help racism, discrimination in general, and civil rights? What is the connection between sexuality, race, gender, and social class in the work(s)?

Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, September 25
Madame Bovary 1215-1249 Shannon: Chapter 5

1) Finish your character sketch to turn in. 2) Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Web CT posts or the idea for Paper 1.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM
- Why does Flaubert become the bias(ed) author when describing the thought process of the characters in his novel? That is, why are the thoughts of his characters subjective rather than objective? What purpose does this serve in terms of the overall structure of the novel?
- How does Flaubert use physcial objects (windows, cigar cases, etc.) to show how Madame Bovary is feeling about her current situation?
- What events, emotion, and/or feelings lead to Emma's instability in every facet of her life and ultimately cause her downfall?
- How does Emma's nature of wanting life to be like a romantic novel have an impact on the novel's outcome?
- What effect do Emma's nature and thoughts about life have on the plot of the story and why does her opinion on what life should be affect what she does throughout the novel?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
Marxist Criticism:
- How do the class differences in Madame Bovary drive the story?
- How are the different levels of social class portrayed in Part 1, Chapter IV, The Wedding? How do the descriptions of the guests’ attire depict which class they belong to?
- Throughout the story, Emma tends to indulge herself with things she can't afford just to make herself happy. Do you think Emma would have found happiness if she would have grown up in an upperclass family with money to afford everything she wanted?
- How do the characters of Madame Bovary display a typical bourgeois lifestyle and in what ways does this tell us about Flaubert's view on the middle class during the mid-19th century?
- In Madame Bovary there are obvious class differences bewteen the characters. How do these class differences support or reject the idea of socialism and social status that Flaubert is trying to portray?
- Consider the relationships between Abbé Bournisien and the other characters. Considering how the priest is initially introduced, portly and still greasy from his last meal, what kind of class commentary is being made using Bournisien? Remember Emma and Hippolyte.

Feminist Criticism:
- Many reasons in the story prevent Emma from carrying out her gender role, such as personality, settings and expectations. What do you think is the main reason for Emma rejecting her gender role? Is it her fantasies that are restraining her? Or do you think this is just another criticism by Flaubert of women not having enough freedom back in that time (freedom in not being allowed to "dream"; always expecting the typical)?
- In Madame Bovary the women seem to accept their roles. Why don't they rebel against this and how do they cope with the unfair female status?
- In Part III of Madame Bovary, Emma gets into a great deal of financial trouble. Her excessive spending at the dry goods store caused to her to owe 8,000 francs. All thoughout the story, Emma complains of boredom. Is Flaubert suggesting the stereotype that women are frivolous is due to the fact that they have nothing to do other than spend money? Is he blaming women for this or is he blaming society for the position it puts women in?
- There seems to be some internal conflict on-going through the novel between Emma's feminine beauty and her wish to assert her dominance over her male counterparts. Is Flaubert making a case for or against women's equality? Emma clearly is no complete role model for women. However, does her dominance (attempted or successful) allow her to be seen as a hero for some women in the time period, or is Flaubert merely making fun of what he assumes is the fantastical and unrealistic mindset of women?
- Why is it that men are considered more free in society? Why are women devalued?
- In the book, all the Madame Bovarys are at times "manly" because they have a power over all the men they encounter. Women weren't usually depicted like this in Flaubert's time. Do you think this is how he wished they were looked at or how they acted? Give examples from the text.
- Why does Emma Bovary like to dress in man-like clothes and wear men's glasses? What does this mean?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM
- What effect does social class and time period have on the morality of humans in Madame Bovary?
- How did society react to Madame Bovary's relationship with her daughter when reading the story?
- What was the general understanding of mental illness during this time period?
- Madame Bovary (1857) was written during the Age of Realism. How are the themes of the age apparent in the text and what are the author's views on Romanticism based on examples from the text?
- How were the gender roles in Madame Bovary portrayed compared to cultural norms in the time period in which it was written?
- How do the benefits of Emma's environment, not having to work, everything handed to her, affect the story and her relationship to others, in particular her relationship with Charles, Leon, and Rodophe?
- If the story were set in the modern day, most likely Emma wouldn't be taking care of all the finances going on between herself and Charles. How would this affect Lheureux's and Emma's plans?
- Taking the French Revolution (1789-1799) and the reign of Napoleon I into consideration, how do you think Flaubert's form and content in Madame Bovary reflect the aftermath of the aforementioned historical times? Consider the collapse of the aristocracy, the rise of the middle class, Flaubert's status as a member of the educated elite, and Emma Bovary's status in society. Did Flaubert write Madame Bovary to agree or disagree with this new middle class at the time?
-Explain Flaubert's view of the middle class during the nineteenth century using examples from the text.

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM
- How did Monsieur and Madame Bovary's parenting style affect Charles' psyche as an adult?
- How does the author use patterns or cycles to reveal characters' unconscious desires?
- What does the reader's reaction to the work say about his or her psychology?
- Flaubert was very critical of the bourgeoisie. How does Flaubert express his criticism of the bourgeoisie through the minds of the characters in Madame Bovary? What point is Flaubert making to the reader by showing us what is going on in their minds?
- Given the romantic symbolism present within a realist script, what can the reader understand about the writer's psychological desires, whether conscious or unconscious?
- Flaubert said, "I am Madame Bovary." Flaubert was an indifferent student (like Charles), found the city distasteful (like Charles), never married, had an affair which upon ending left him with a loss of interest in romance, yet he suffered from venereal disease most of his life (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert), which indicates that he was promiscuous. [Related to his visits to prostitutes. Dr. B] Since Emma's character seems to mimic many of Flaubert's own personal characteristics, do you think Flaubert was relating to us his personal struggle with inner psychological demons?
- Why does he invent Berthe and Mme Rollet whose appearances are so few and far between?
- How does Emma's perception of her life and the end of the day (whether consciously or subconsciously) reflect the author's psychology about hope's presence in reality? Does the author's life up to the point he wrote the book reflect any of the characters' psychological motives and their outcomes?
- Emma is hysterical after losing the power of attorney. Why is she laughing after this upsetting event?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM
- After reading the different assigned section of Madame Bovary, what feelings are evoked by these passages? Also, do you find yourself relating to different parts of the story? Give examples from the story to support your answer.
- Are there any situations in your life that can connect you to any of these characters? If so, which character(s)?
- Who is Madame Bovary's intended audience? In what ways is the modern reader like or unlike that original audience?
- Which character can you relate most to and why?

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM
- How does Emma's environment mirror or affect her character?
- Why does the garden prove to be a prominent symbol for Emma?
- Flaubert often describes his characters in reference to the surrounding nature and animals such as a horse. If the setting were placed in a modern city, would the descriptions come across as powerfully? What alternatives to descriptions such as horses could be used in our technologically advanced culture?

• QUEER THEORY
- What role does the characters' identification with their sexuality play into the plot of Madame Bovary? How does sexuality affect the actions of the main characters?
- Throughout the story, Madame Bovary has affairs with men. What feelings does this evoke in you?
- In developing Queer Theory, were individuals seen a individuals or just parts of a society as a whole? Also, by suggesting that we look more upon individuals, how does this change one's view of society as a whole?
- How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sex to define others? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism?
- How does Emma's behavior on her wedding night with Charles translate into her future affairs with other men?
-Why do you think Emma is promiscuous? Is it because she wants to act like a man?

2) Also: "The Oedipus complex in Freudian psychoanalysis refers to stage of psychosexual development in childhood where children of both sexes regard their father as an adversary and competitor for the exclusive love of their mother. The name of the complex derives from the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unwittingly kills his father Laius and marries his mother Jocasta.

Freud considered the successful resolution of the Oedipus complex to be key to the development of gender roles and identity, with boys and girls resolving the conflicts differently as a result of castration anxiety and penis envy. Freud also held that the unsuccessful resolution of the Oedipus complex could result in neurosis and homosexuality.… Although common usage refers to 'suffering from an Oedipus complex', psychoanalysis does not consider the complex a pathology, but instead a perfectly normal stage that all children go through. Oedipal desires are thought to remain heavily repressed and unconsciously in the minds of all functioning adults."

• FORMALIST CRITICISM

- Why is the bug not a specific species?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
Marxist Criticism:
- How do economic conditions determine the characters' lives?
- How does the first half of The Metamorphosis illustrate how the economic conditions affect the life of Gregor Samsa and his family?
- Does Gregory's lack of income mean that it is his fault that his family is struggling?
- Gregory's family is obviously reliant on his income. How does their actual social class affect how they treat him when he becomes a monstruous vermin?
- Does the work criticize class structure?

Feminist Criticism:
- Why is Gregory required to work when his sister simply stays at home? Gregory works to provide for his family and doesn't want his sister to have to work, even those his family is in debt and could use the money.
- How does Kafka exhibit the mother in the story?
- Do you think the women in this story are depicted in an honorable and courageous light? Or do you think they are just doing it because they think they have to, but really their heart isn't in the right place?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM
- How many different ways are cultural norms represented through the actions of the characters and story line of this literary work?
- How does the work reflect the period it was written in as far as parental expectations are concerned?
- How does the work reflect the period in which it was created?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM
- How does the work reflect the author’s personal psychology?
- How does Gregory's attitude about being a beetle change throughout the first two sections of The Metamorphosis?
- What do the actions and thoughts of Gregor say about the author's actual personality?
- What do the character's emotions and behavior reveal about their psychological states? What types of personalities are they?
- How does Gregory's reaction to his condition reflect his character?
- How does the behavior of Gregory's family affect him after the "metamorphosis'?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM
- Who would want to be an insect?
- Why did Kafka choose to make Gregor a bug?
- How does Gregory's condition make you feel about him as a character?
- What question is left most unanswered through the first half of the novella?
- How do you respond to this work? What feelings are aroused?
- After reading the first half of the story, how does Gregor's treatment by his family before and after his transformation make you feel?
- After reading the assigned section of The Metamorphosis, what feelings are evoked by these passages? Also, do you find yourself relating to different parts of the story?
- Which character can you relate to most and why? (See the quiz: Dr. B)
- When we have one or many of what is call a metamorphosis, we feel them on such an extremely personal level. Depending on the circumstance of one’s childhood do we have a tendency to confuse the rational and irrational as they intertwine?
