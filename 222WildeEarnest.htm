Stories based on Oscar Wilde's The Importance of Being Earnest

Mr. Bunbury has a Cold
by Mark Humphrey

            Algy held his daily whiskey sour and brooded.  There are times in a man's life when trouble rears its ugly head, and the afore mentioned man must stop in his tracks and brood over the outcome of what surely must be disaster.  Lane, Algy's butler entered and began to tidy the sitting room.  Over the years, Lane had noticed that within ten minutes of the usual drink, Algy would begin to talk about whatever it was that bothered him.  Today had a particularly difficult problem and Lane had given his master twenty minutes.

            "It's a bit thick, I mean." Algy began.

            "Sir?"

            "I mean Aunt Julia pushing this stranger onto us.  The American chap."

            "Are you not inclined to have him stay, sir?"

            "No.  You remember the last person she told me to put up for the week.  That Swiss millionaire friend of hers that turned out to be a Marxist agitator.  Nearly burnt down the flat!"

            "Yes sir, I recall the incident quite clearly.  I believe I read recently he is currently undergoing observation at an institution. Might I take the liberty and inquire whom she wishes us to entertain, sir?"

            "You may well ask.  His name is Roderick P. Simpson.  Apparently his mum and Aunt Julia are pals.  He's in London and the dearest A asked me to put him up for the fortnight.  Bally nuisance.  I heard he's some kind of playboy in the home country which means he'll be a playboy here and drag me along for the ride!"

            "Yes sir.  During our vacation in America not long ago, I chanced to read a newspaper article devoted to the young gentleman.  It did not speak highly of him."

            "I shouldn't wonder.  He's most likely well known to the Police.  I'll come home one evening to find its standing room only with the local constabulary."

            "The newspaper article I alluded to endorses your opinion, sir."

Lane was about to try his best to console the master when a knock was heard on the front door.  Soon after, he returned to introduce Mr. George Wellbeloved and Lord Wittly, two of Algy's closest friends.

            "I say, what ho Algy!" George beamed.

            "George!  And I see you dragged out Wittly!"

            Lord Wittly finished tripping over a small settee and joined the group.

            "I say, I'm awfully dry, can I have one of those?" Witty pointed at the whiskey sour. 

            "Whiskey sours all 'round, Lane."

            "Very good, sir." Lane disappeared into the kitchen.

            "So young Wellbeloved, what brings you to my humble doorstep?"

            "Well, Wittly and I are about to travel down to my Aunt's place in Cornwall.  We had some news you might be interested in."

            "Cornwall?  Why on Earth are you going there?" Algy sat back down and lit a cigarette.

            "My Aunt wants me to hand out the prizes and make a speech at the local school award ceremony.  Someone must have told her about that whopping great pile of cash I won on "Midnight Mistress" down at Ascot."

            "That or your affiliation with the political party." Lord Wittly cut in.

            "Doesn't matter.  Anyway, I've got to make a speech and hand out horrible prizes to a horde of rug rats.  There is a bright side though.  Algy, do you still have the sporting blood?"

            "My dear fellow, the only blood I have is sporting." Algy replied.

            "Perfect.  It's like this.  After the prizes are handed out, there is a sort of festival taking place on my Aunt's estate.  Lots of sack races, egg tosses, you get the picture."

            "And where is all this going?"

            "Well, Lord Wittly and I have formed a sort of consortium with the lads down at the club.  Nothing official, but I'll tell you that it’s a dead certainty that little Katie Turner will win the girl's egg toss."

            "Are you suggesting we bet on children's games?"

            "Well, why not?"

            "Right then.  Put a couple of tenners on Katie's nose for me.  I'm stuck in London."

            "What?  We need a third person down there with ready cash!  I could easily get you invited to my Aunt's estate!"

            "Yes, we need a third person!  While the other two are watching the other events, the third would watch his assigned event!" Lord Wittly added.

            "Watch for any nobling of the contestants?  They would stoop so low in such a contest?"

            "Of course!  Purity of the turf means nothing in these rural festival games.  I imagine the bookie is already feeding chocolate to the favourite for the choirboy's ten yard dash.  When we get there, we'll need a third to help us decide on duds and winners." George lamented.

            "Still, my Aunt Julia has offloaded some American blighter on me.  I can't just leave him.  He'll tell Aunt Julia and she'll come down like a flash.  You know how she snarls."

            "What are we going to do?" George flung his hands in the air.

            There was silence as the minds turned over the vexing problems of the world.

            "I say, I have a capital idea!" Lord Wittly exclaimed.

            "Last summer, " he continued, "My beastly Uncle decided to drag me with him to his annual Brighton holiday.  At the last minute one of his old friends came down with a nasty cold, saving me from almost certain boredom."

            "You mean he postponed the trip to see his friend?" George asked.

            "Exactly."

            "I think I see what you're getting at." Algy's mind began to turn.

            "You could have a sick cousin, or uncle, or even a friend.  Just make up someone to go see!" George smiled.

            "He would have to be a close friend.  He's very ill." Wittly said.

            "Lane!" Algy called.

            "Sir?" Lane appeared and handed out the needful beverages.

            "Lane, an old friend of mine is sick.  I'm afraid I'm going to have to go see him.  He's come down with….a cold."

            "A very nasty cold.  He's got frail health you know." Cut in George.

            "Yes, frail health and all that.  I'm afraid that when Mr. Simpson arrives I shall not be able to join him in London.  I'm sure Aunt Julia will understand?"

            Lane's eyebrow rose an inch.  He looked at his master, then George, and then at Lord Wittly, who merely smiled and waved.  A twitch on the right side of Lane's lips occurred.

            "Yes sir, I shall inform Mr. Simpson and her ladyship that you have gone to see Mr….Mr. Bunbury, a dear old friend with frail health residing in the country.  I'm sure her ladyship will not mind you absence in light of the situation."

            "In York, Lane."

            "Yes sir, in York."

            "Thank you Lane.  Oh, and please pack my things for a trip to the country."

            "Yes sir, I believe the tweed suit we purchased yesterday should be appropriate for travel."

            "Thank goodness for the ill health of Mr….who was it again, Lane?"

            "Bunbury, sir.  Your friend's name is Mr. Bunbury."
