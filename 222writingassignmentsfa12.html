    Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Monday, September 24

    Finish reading Madame Bovary 1215-1249.

    Write a summary of Chapter 7 in A Short Guide to turn in.

    Other writing assignments:

    1. Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Book III?
    2. Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

      3. FORMALIST CRITICISM:

      • Flaubert changes the tempo of Madame Bovary by describing certain events in great detail, others are briefly told. How does the form of Madame Bovary reflect this?
      • How does the binary opposition between Emma and Charles reenforce the moral of Madame Bovary?

      SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

      Marxist Criticism:

      • Is Emma solely responsible for her problem, or is she a victim of society? Regardless, is it easy to sympathize with her? Why or why not?
      • What do Lheureux's actions say about the Capitalist economic system, and what does Emma and Charles' response to his actions show about Captialism's effects on society?

      Feminist Criticism:

      • In what ways does Emma reject typical women's roles? What are her views on these roles? Is Emma a feminist or is she just unhappy?
      • How does Emma's character compare to other women in this time period? Is she an accurate representation of women in this time period?
      • How does Flaubert depict his overall message through the death of Charles and Emma? Could Emma's suicide been foreseen earlier in the story?

      CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

      • How do Emma's fantasies of a cheating wife tell us about the cultural and historical references of the time? Was it the work of a realist woman? Was it the sign of a cultural change in progress rooted in the idea of realism?
      • What historical events influenced Flaubert's life and led to an influence in his writing of Madame Bovary? For example, why was he so against the middle-class and their values?

      PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

      • What does Flaubert's portrayal of Emma's depression and discontent with her husband illustrate about the author's personality?
      • What do Emma's behavior and actions show about her psychological state? What is the author trying to convey by making her character the way that it is?
      • Does Flaubert present psychological matters within Emma's character like dreams, repression, or desire? Is Emma conscious or unconscious when these matters are presented?

      READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

      • Can you see yourself living during this time period? What are some aspects of life you can see yourself doing/not doing during this time?

      ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

      • How does the relationship between Emma and Rodolphe and their social environment affect the characters, plot and theme?
      • How do you think Flaubert uses Nature to shape his characters in the story?

      QUEER THEORY:

      • Considering Emma and her relationships, does Madame Bovary reflect love and beauty or marriage and reproduction?
      • Could Emma's deisre for passion from men be blamed on her upbringing by a single father?
      • Would you agree that this novel contains an immense amount of sexuality? If so, would you agree or consider the fact that Emma may have a serious issue psychologically due to her personal and constant desires and needs for satisfaction in life through men and sex? (She really shows a never-ending dissatisfaction with her life; she constantly needs attention, luxurious items, men, sex, to feel good, to be accepted, to be rich, to be famous, to have the world at the palm of her hand, and so on.) Would you then agree that she may be narcissistic and may be infatuated with herself suggesting homosexuality?

    • How does reading the story from Gregor's perspective affect the reader? How would this story be different if it were told from another point of view?
    • How does Gregory's casual view of his situation affect the reader's perspective as they grapple with the concept of a human insect?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • How do economic conditions determine the characters' lives in The Metamorphosis?
    • How does Gregor's transformation change his famiy in economic and emotional ways now that they realize that they can't be dependent on him and in fact he is now dependent on them?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • How does the title relate to feminist literature?
    • How does living in a patriarchal society affect the women in the story? How do you know it is a patriarchal society?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • What role, if any, did World War I play in the development of The Metamorphosis or the Expressionist movement?
    • What are some of the characteristics of cultural and historical change that take place in the story?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Franz Kafka resented his father's overbearing nature and felt deprived of maternal love (1964). Do you think this is why he wrote the story and made himself become an insect? Or do you think Kafka had other intentions in writing The Metamorphosis?
    • What, if anything, do Gregor's actions after the metamorphosis reveal about his feelings toward himself and his family prior to the change?
    • How does the work reflect the author's personal psychology?
    • How did Kafka's life translate into the metamophosis? What are similar themes?
    • How does Kafka present Gregor's psychological matters such a repressions, dreams, and desire either consciously or unconsciously?

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • Similar to Gregor's condition, have you ever felt like "the bug" in the room or in a situation with either peers or family? In your opinion, why is Gregor so alientated from his family and other people in general?
    • How did you respond to the opening scene of the story and what did it set you up to expect from the remainder of the story?
    • What do you think this story illustrates about Kafka's life?
    • Why is there no reason given for his transformation?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • How does Gregory's phsycial transformation mimic the claustrophobic environment he is bound to in the story?
    • Why out of all the possible insects or animals that Gregor could transform into does he turn into a cockroach-like bug? How doe the rest of the characters respond to his transformation and how does the fact that he is now a cockroach affect what he is fed and his lifestyle?

    QUEER THEORY:

    • How does the way Gregory's family treats him as an outcast after he becomes a vermin similar to the way family and friends react to someone after they coming out about being homosexual?

    • There is a blend of gender roles in the context, form, and title of this work. How does Gregor show that he feel inadequate in his masculine role and through this metamorphosis seek to live as a woman?

    Highlights include:

    -Step 3: Reflect on your peer reviewers' suggestions and write a full draft of your paper to turn in to me. Proofread following the Editing and other checklists listed on the inside cover of A Short Guide. I will not grade the paper at this point but will return it to you with comments, so that you can write the final draft. If you are doing a creative project, be sure you have included both the creative section and the critical or artist's journal section. Consult the Scoring Rubric for Papers, Scoring Rubric for Creative Paper, and Scoring Rubric for Creative Art Projects so that you'll know what I will be looking for when I do grade the paper.

    • Chapter 14 in A Short Guide is an exellent presentation of the style and format I expect in a literary paper. Pay attention to good organization and logical transitions between paragraphs.
    • Base your arguments on close readings of the texts you have chosen.

    Two areas that are normally weak in the first draft are the use of quotations and the conclusion.

    • Give concrete examples (quotes) from the texts to support your arguments, but not to fill up space. These quotations are the evidence you need to provide to prove your thesis. You will usually use at least one in each of body paragraphs.
    • In order to integrate quotes appropriately, first introduce the quote by making the point, connected to your thesis, that the passage illustrates. Next, include the quote, usually 3-5 lines of text. Finally, tie the quote back into your argument with a concluding sentence in your own words: "…remind your reader that these passages are there to prove a point. End your discussion on your words, not theirs." (Shannon, 162-3).

    A conclusion is very important to a fully developed paper. Go beyond the ideas in your introduction. Have you proved your thesis? All readers, including your professor, will ask the question: "So what?" The conclusion should make the relevance and importance of your research clear.

    You are not required to use secondary sources (books, articles, internet) for this paper, but if you do, be sure to quote and document them properly and attach a “Works Cited” page. Plagiarism is a poor choice and will not be tolerated; consult the Course Information page about the NC State academic integrity policy; ask me if questions remain. For information on using research (if you do) and writing the documentation page, consult A Short Guide, chapter 17.
