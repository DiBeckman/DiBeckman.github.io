II. Translation difficulties

Verbs such as attendre, chercher, écouter, and regarder take a direct object in French but not in English. (For extra practice and since Chris and Will did it this way in class, I've included the same sentences with the nouns replaced by pronouns in parentheses.)

1. Paul is waiting for Cécile. Paul attend Cécile. (Il l'attend.)

2. The girls watch the movie. Les filles regardent le film. Elles le regardent.)

3. Amélie is looking for Nino. Amélie cheche Nino. (Elle le cherche.)

4. The students are listening to Céline Dion. Les étudiants écoutent Céline Dion. (Ils l'écoutent.)

Others verbs that may cause translation problems with direct and indirect objects: parler, connaître, inviter, envoyer

5. She invites students to her house. Elle invite les étudiants chez elle. (Elle les y invite.)

6. I know my mother well. Je connais bien ma mère. (Je la connais bien.)

7. He talks to his uncles and they talk to their cousins. Il parle à ses oncles et ils parlent à leurs cousins.
(Il leur parle et ils leur parlent.)

8. She sends her students an email. Elle envoie un mail à ses étudiants. (direct object comes before indirect object in French.) (Elle leur en envoie un.)

VIII. Adjectifs possessifs
In French, the possessive adjectives, like all adjectives, agree in gender (male/female) and number (singular/ plural) with the noun they modify. It may seem odd to English speakers that the adjective agrees with the noun it modifies rather than the person who posseses the object, but that's another way French and English are different.

Example:
She loves her sweater. Elle adore son pull-over.
He gets along well with his sister. Il s'entend bien avec sa sœur.
