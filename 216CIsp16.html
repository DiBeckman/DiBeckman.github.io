COURSE DESCRIPTION

France: Art and Society is a new 3-credit Spring Semester Course which meets on campus through the semester and also includes a week (Spring Break) spent in Paris, France. This class will introduce students to the history of French Art as well as the French language (a major influence on English). By looking at the role Paris played and still plays on the world stage and focusing on themes of diversity and power, we will investigate how studying another country's art and society will help us to better understand our own.

"If you really want to see something, look at something else." —Howard Nemerov. To understand European and American history, understanding Paris is essential. This class will compare the French and American cultures though the visual and performing arts, introduce students to the French language (a major influence on English), and give them the opportunity to examine in writing their own "in-group" and social identity. By selecting and then speaking and writing about four Images of Power, students will "engage the human experience through the interpretation of human culture [and] become aware of the act of interpretation itself as a critical form of knowing in the humanities." (General Education Program, NCSU) This course meets GEP Visual and Performing Arts and Global Knowledge requirements as FL 216 and meets the GEP Visual and Performing Arts and US Diversity requirements as VPUS 295.

There are no prerequisites for the course. The language of instruction is English. If you are interested in becoming proficient in French, you might sign up for FLF 101 or FLF 110 as well. There will be intensive French tourist language instruction as part of on-campus, pre-departure classes. Students proficient in French will be able to submit the final paper in French.

Student Learning Outcomes

Visual & Performing Arts

At the end of the course students will be able to:

  • Describe the aesthetic, historical, and cultural evolution of the arts in France.
  • Analyze works of French of art to demonstrate how the general social and historical background is embedded in the artistic details of the chosen work of art and how artistic elements (composition, color, line, space, shapes, etc.) are used to create content and meaning.
  • Explain how French art relates to the cultural and historical contexts in the country of France.
Global Knowledge

At the end of the course students will be able to:

  • Identify and examine works of art, cultural artifacts, ideas, values, technological developments, and attitudes of France from the country's origins to the present.
  • Explain how French art relates to its cultural and historical contexts in the country of France.

Diversity

  • Identify, research, and discuss in writing a specific sociological "in-group" reflecting the American experience of religious, gender, ethnic, racial, class, sexual orientation, disability, and/or age identities as shaped by our cultural and societal influences.
  • Evaluate and interpret evidence of difference and culture, identity and community, privilege and oppression, and power and responsibility in student-selected images (fine arts or popular culture) from the United States and France.

Rubric for entries about reading & films
Please include the following information for each entry:

1. Just the Facts
Title:
Author/ Director:
Date:
Main Characters: with a one sentence description of each.

2. Summary: one-three paragraphs.
For fiction: What happens? For nonfiction: What is the main argument of the work?

3. Appreciation and Insights

How would you rate this work? What comments do you have about the style, content, uniqueness of this work?

What are your observations and cultural or historical insights related to our diversity themes: difference and culture, identity and community, privilege and oppression, and power and responsibility, including race and ethnicity, gender, class, immigration…?

  • What did you learn about Paris? What did you learn about France? What did you learn about the US?
  • What did you learn about the historical period?
  • Cultural Values?
  • Does this work lead you to think about American history or culture in a new way?

Additional questions to consider for films:

  • How does the title function? Is the English title simply a translation of the French?
  • Determine the dominant kinds of shots in a film (close-up, medium shot, long shot, etc.) and consider how these shots affect your responses to the characters and/or action depicted?
  • Does the camera move or remain stationary? How do camera movements or lack of them affect your responses?
  • How does the visual information (ie. details of the frame) of an image determine meaning?
  • How long are the shots in a particular film, and how do long takes or rapid montages affect our responses?
  • How does the editing (way in which shots are joined together) affect your emotional and/or intellectual responses?
  • How does the music affect your emotional response in particular sequences?
  • Consider the effectiveness of the actors in films. For instance, some actors are brilliant in theater but not always so in film. Why?
  • Consider the relationships of sound to the images.
  • Do the costumes just look “nice,” or do they help support characterization and/or theme?

AUTOETHNOGRAPHY and Taking on Diversity

January 14: Brief Introduction to Allport's "The Formation of In-groups"; Taking on Diversity, Introduction and Part 1
January 21: Discussion of Allport's article; Taking on Diversity, Part 2
January 28: Introduction to research methods, Taking on Diversity, Part 3
February 4: Read sample scholarly & student autoethnographies; Taking on Diversity, Part 4
February 11: Topic proposal for Autoenthnography due; Taking on Diversity, Part 5
February 18: Taking on Diversity, Part 6
February 25: Taking on Diversity, Part 7
March 3: Taking on Diversity, Part 8
March 24: First draft due/peer review of Autoenthography
April 7: Final draft due

You belong to many different kinds of social groups, both voluntarily and involuntarily. These groups help you to understand and define who you are, who you are becoming, and what your unique role in our society is. For this course, you will write an autoethnography as a way for you to explore, analyze, and explain how one of the groups that you belong to has affected your sense of who you are or who you are becoming. Autoethnography is a genre of writing that explores the researcher's personal experience and connects this autobiographical story to wider cultural, political, and social meanings and understandings through qualitative research.

In this autoethnography, you will explore your position within your in-group, broaden your audience's understanding of the group, and address challenges that you and your group may face. Though this assignment is very personal and reflective in nature, you will need to do more than simply tell your audience about yourself and your in-group. An autoethnography is not just a personal narrative or story; thus, you will need to use your collected research in order to help you analyze the social movements and relationships of your group with some degree of depth.

CLASS DISCUSSIONS POLICY

We will have open and honest discussions of race and culture, including interracial or intercultural interactions, misunderstandings and confusions. To make our discussion authentic, we will not attack each other. And we will pledge not to take anyone’s name outside of this classroom. Leave here and talk about what was said, so that you can understand it better. But leave the classroom with no person’s name (except the professors') on your lips. In other words, who says what in here, stays in here.

DIVERSITY POLICY

This course fosters free and open dialogue, the acceptance and discussion of different opinions, and mutual respect among class members. Since class participation is required, it is important that students not be afraid to speak up and share their ideas. If you ever feel that you have been intimidated or treated disrespectfully in class, by me or by another student, please come see me in private as soon as possible. That is not the environment I desire for this class.

Every member of the university community has the right to work and learn in an environment free from discrimination and harassment. Since everyone has this right, everyone also has the responsibility to help prevent discrimination or harassment from occurring. One of the most important ways is to respect others and make sure your actions serve as a role model for others. You can also:

  • Get to know those around you.
  • Our literary texts discuss sensitive topics such as sex, race, ethnicity, and religion. Your observations on these topics should relate to the works we are studying and your responses to them. If the one of these topics triggers an intense personal reaction, don't hesitate to come speak to me or a University counselor privately about it.
  • Refrain from using jokes, words, and gestures with sexual, racial, religious, or other discriminatory connotations.
  • Respect others' personal space.
  • Avoid making assumptions about others. Ask for their opinions and insights.
  • As members of a university community, we all have the right to work and learn without being discriminated against or harassed. We also have some responsibilities — to help prevent discrimination and harassment, and to help address it when it does occur.
