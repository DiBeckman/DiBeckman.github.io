LEARNING OUTCOMES

At the end of this course, students will be able to:

  1. use learned grammatical structures to speak and write in elementary or novice-level French;
  2. create written and oral sentences using learned vocabulary;
  3. read French at the elementary or novice level;
  4. understand spoken French at the elementary or novice level;
  5. identify aspects of the cultures of the French-speaking world.

WELCOME TO FRENCH!

This course aims at giving you a solid foundation in understanding, speaking, reading and writing the French language at the elementary level. To accomplish these goals, it is essential that we use French as the language of the classroom. Handy phrases for communicating in French:

Répétez, s'il vous plaît = Repeat, please.
Une minute, s'il vous plaît. = Wait a minute, please.
Comment dit-on [English word] en français = How do you say [English word] in French?
Que veut dire [French word]?= What does [French word] mean?
Qu'est-ce que c'est? = What's that?
Je ne sais pas. = I don't know.
Je ne comprends pas. = I don't understand.

Although the language of instruction and interaction in my classroom is French, I do not expect you to understand every single word I am saying. Do not hesitate to let me know if your confusion gets in the way of class participation. I will find another way of getting my point across, whether it is by repeating, paraphrasing, elaborating, or simply slowing down. Raising your hand in class is the best way to let me know you have a question. Please come see me after class or during my office hours! Every student will benefit from one-to-one time with me whether you have questions about grammar, pronunciation, or simply to practice speaking French.

HOW TO DO WELL IN CLASS

  1. Prepare assignments before coming to class so we can get the most out of our class time together. Assignments are to be completed as indicated on the syllabus. I expect you to have studied the grammar points as explained in the textbook and completed the homework before coming to class with any questions. In class, I will be presenting the same material in French as a review before we do face-to-face exercises (teacher-student and student-student) in class. Some students find it helpful to read written explanations in both Motifs and English Grammar for Students of French before coming to class, for difficult grammar points. Others consult general French grammar web sites such as Tex's French Grammar and others listed on the Supplementary Materials page. Part of my job is to help you find the approach that keeps you in the stretch zone and works well for you, depending on your learning style (see Extra Credit Assignment on Myers-Briggs Type in College). We are most likely to figure this out together during a tutoring session in my office, so don't hesitate to come see me.
  2. Homework is essential. You will have daily homework. It is necessary to do the homework to succeed on the exams. There is a significant amount of work for this class. It is important to keep up and impossible to cram for this course and do well. You will not be able to absorb all the grammar points from classroom instruction alone. That is why there is homework, a lab manual, resources on the Cengage site, and my Supplementary Materials page. Learning a language is like building a house of cards; each one must be well in place before adding the next. Getting behind can lead to serious difficulties including an F in the class. Spend at least 30 minutes on French per day. Regular repetition of small quantities of information is the most successful method for learning a foreign language.
  3. Participate actively in class. Mentally answer each and every question when a classmate is responding. Class participation and homework make up 25% of your grade. The more you participate, the better your grade will be, and the better your French will become. Your class grade reflects your performance in class (attendance, knowledge of daily lesson, volunteering answers, asking questions). Take listening comprehension seriously — it will help both your proficiency and your grade. There will be a listening component on all exams.
  4. Take risks. Speak out. Don’t be afraid to make mistakes. I am here to help. The more you speak, the more you will get out of the class. Save perfection for exams. The desire to communicate is the most important consideration when speaking.
  5. Speak French as much as possible in the classroom. Speak French outside the classroom whenever possible, too. Listen to French music, go to French movies, eat French food!
  6. Do not chew gum in class. Having something in your mouth makes it harder to pronounce French correctly.
  7. Bring Motifs to class every day. This is essential. Bring a portable French/English-English/French dictionary to class, if you find it useful. Alternatively, you can consult an online resource such as http://wordreference.com or ask me for vocabulary assistance in class.
  8. Take notes in class. I can put any poster that I put on the board up on my Supplemenatary Materials page, if it isn't already there. Buy 3x5 notecards to make personalized verb charts and other study aids. I like to use colored index cards with pink for feminine nouns and blue for masculine. Study with a friend outside of class. There are excellent study tips in English Grammar for Students of French.
  9. Cell phones and mobile devices must be silent and out of sight during class. If you need to know what time it is, you may ask me (in French, of course: Quelle heure est-il?). No texting during class. If I see you use your phone during class, it will count against your participation grade.

I will do my best to keep the class atmosphere creative, lively, and accepting of your best efforts. Both effort and ability will be rewarded. In return, you, too have some responsibilities. Please read carefully and follow the advice above and the information given below.

HOMEWORK

Each module of the textbook is divided into two parts: 1) White Pages (Thèmes, Pratiques de conversation, Perspectives culturelles, Écoutons ensemble, Lecture, Expression écrite) and 2) Green Pages (Structures utiles).

Before each class, look through the white pages (column 2 of the syllabus) so you know what we will be doing in class. The audio for each Module (indicated by the speaker graphic) is included on the CDs included with the textbook as well as the Cengage web site. Listening to the exercises and the vocabulary will help you get used to hearing French.

The third column in the syllabus lists selected Lecture: reading exercises. There are written exercises to turn in listed on the syllabus (Compréhension et intégration; Et vous? Avez-vous compris?) Answers to these exercises do not appear in the Answer Key, so I will correct these for you. In the first few modules, the questions are in English, so you can answer in English. Later, the questions will be in French. If you are having trouble with these questions, try writing out the question and translating it into English. I'll correct that, too. If you are struggling with the readings, we can go over them together during an office hour. Reading in French is an important skill you may not have practiced much before this class.

Your main homework is to write out the answers to exercises in the green pages (column 4 of the syllabus). You may choose to do the even or odd questions for each exercise, except for Tout ensemble, where you should do all. It is also a good idea to translate the French words in the word bank into English before trying to do Tout ensemble, since it is the concluding excercise for the Module and will help ensure you know the relevant vocabulary and grammar points.

Check this work using Appendix B, page 485 ff. in the textbook. All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. If there are no errors in your homework, put a check mark by each section so I know that you've corrected your work. When you fill in answers from the Answer Key, be sure to do so in your correction color. If you have questions about an exercise, write me a note on your homework. Here is a sample homework that is appropriately corrected.

Reminder: copying answers from the textbook or workbook answer key without trying them on your own first, is not only a waste of time (what would you learn by copying?), but also an Academic Integrity violation (see above). If you have no idea what the exercise is about, it makes sense to do one or two questions and check the answers to see what is expected. Try your best. You will never be graded on the number of errors in your homework or workbook. Mistakes are normal when you are learning, and I expect to see mistakes and corrections on all of your homework. Revising and correcting your own errors is the best way to ensure that you do not make the same errors on a test. Helping you learn to notice and correct your own errors is one of my pedagogical goals in this class.

You will turn in the corrected work from the green pages daily and all other written assignments as indicated on the syllabus. Please put the page or Module section numbers at the top of each homework assignment (for example, Module 1.1-1.3, 24-27). Full credit will be given only for corrected work turned in on time.

You will also do the exercises in the Workbook/Lab Manual corresponding to each Module. All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. The answers are in the Workbook Answer Key. For copyright reasons, I have posted it on our Moodle site: If there are no errors, put a check mark by each exercise so I know at a glance that you've corrected your work. Mark any sections you don't understand so that you can ask about them in class or I can explain when I'm correcting your work.

In the first half of the Workbook, you may do even or odd questions in each exercise, but you must do each exercise. Except for Module 5, you will skip Synthèse, as indicated on the Syllabus. In the second half of the Workbook/Lab manual, you need to do all the questions. Suggested completion dates are indicated on the Syllabus, however, I will not collect these exercises until the Module exam. Bring your Workbook/Lab Manual to each exam so that I can give you credit for your work. You may also write your answers on a separate sheet of paper as long as it is clearly labeled. You will get full credit (10 points) for complete and corrected exercises, and partial credit (up to 5 points) for uncorrected exercises, incomplete, or late work.

Listening Comprehension and Pronunciation Exercises in the Lab Manual (second half of the workbook) are to be completed with the audio available online, either on the Cengage site or our Moodle site.

In additional to the daily homework, you will also read and complete the self-correcting exercises in English Grammar for Students of French. Assigned sections for each exam are listed on the Syllabus. Some chapters do not have associated exercises. Use the Review Exercises booklet to find both exercises and the answer key. The completed and corrected exercises are due each exam day for full credit (5 points).

COMPOSITIONS

Expression écrite and Synthèse: You will write a double-spaced, handwritten or word-processed composition for each Module, submitting a first draft and final version for each. The due date for the first draft is indicated on the syllabus, and there is a penalty of 5 points for late compositions. Learn to type accents on your computer. You will find an explanation of how to do this on the Supplementary Materials page (see Accents).

Drafts will be returned with corrections indicated. Beginning with Module 3, I will be giving you a grade on revised compositions. Unrevised compositions will receive a check mark which corresponds to a 75 when I calculate the final grade. Compositions will be graded using the Writing Assessment Rubric.

When you use a dictionary, make sure that your word fits the context in French. For example: the noun match in English may correspond to un match in French (a sporting event) or une allumette (used to light a fire). Context matters. For more information about how to use a bilingual foreign language dictionary, see the link to the Dictionary Skills Resource Pack in the previous section. Reminder: do not use on-line translators for writing assignments for more than individual words or phrases. Do use your dictionary or http://www.wordreference.com/
