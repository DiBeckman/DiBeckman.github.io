    Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, September 23

    Finish reading Madame Bovary 1215-1249.

    First museum visit tonight at 7 pm.

    Writing assignments

    1. Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Book III?
    2. Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

    FORMALIST CRITICISM:

    • What do you think about Flaubert's usage of description? Are his descriptions just right or is he inundating the reader with unnecessary details?
    • This story uses a third-person omnisicent point of view. Would any other point of view be sufficient for this or any other realist story? Explain why or why not.
    • How does Flaubert use irony throughout Madame Bovary regarding Emma's love life and her love for romanticism?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • Is Emma's deep desire to be a part of the higher class a reflection of her need for attention?
    • Is Emma's longing for life in the upper class, as well as the small and teasing tastes of it the author's way of making fun of class structure? Or simply to draw the reader's attention to it?
    • What tone is set in portraying class difference in Madame Bovary and how is it shown?
    • Is Flaubert trying to portray that even though our society is evolving toward classes of the very rich and the very poor, or does a definitive middle class still exist?
    • How important are the economic factors in a character's life in determining the social status of the individual in addition to determining how the character is viewed by others? How is this idea changed by the influx or loss of economic status?
    • Do you think that if the social and economic classes were cut out of Madame Bovary, that the book would be too greatly reduced, if not obsolete?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • It seems as if Flaubert sees financial situations a matters to be the burden of a man, and a task which women cannot easily handle. What other story written close to this time also portrays this idea? Also what is Flaubert's objective in making Emma so materialistic and "man-like?"
    • Her seeming desire to be a man seems to drive her relationships, but does being a man have anything to do with being free?
    • Does her treatment of her daughter on page 1105 make her seem somehow less feminine? Or even human?
    • Why does she reject her female role so fiercely? A desire to explore? For money? Or something else?
    • When she finds out she is pregnant, why is she only excited to buy things, not for her child?
    • As a woman living in a very gender-divided society, how does Emma possess power?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • Based on the actions and bahviors of Emma, Charles, and Homais, how does Flaubert potray the period of French history known as the "July Monarchy'? What does this say about Flaubert's attitude toward the Bourgeoisie and their impact on this period of French history? Looking at French history, what was the extent of the impact of the bourgeoisie on French culture, aside from Flaubert's opinions?
    • How much is the time Emma lived in to blame for all her problems? Specifically, what are some examples of the type of society she lived in that influenced her actions?
    • If the story behind Madame Bovary had occured during a later time period, today for example, do you think Emma would have acted differently? Why or why not?
    • Is Emma's behavior the norm of this period or is it something only limited to her due to her upbringing?
    • Flaubert's Madame Bovary is set in 19th century France. How does the novel reflect this time period? Also, the culture of France plays a very important part of the plot of the novel. Do you think the novel would be different had it been set in another country, for example, America?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Psychologically, how can you describe Emma's state of being? Do you think there is one psychological element from Emma's personality that stands out from these in your previous answer?
    • What events in Emma's life do you suppose led her to constantly desire change?
    • Throughout the story we often see Emma struggling with her emotions when it comes to love. Things don't work out with her and Charles, her and Rodolphe, or her and Leon. It is clear that Emma deeply desires to have love of the kind that she reads about in her romantic novels. Do you think Emma really wants to find that kind of love one day, in the form of a companion? Or would she really be more content with just the thought of love? Maybe she feels that in her mind she can have it the way she wants, without a man doing something to mess things up.

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • As Emma and Leon's romantic affair flourishes behind Charle's back, how does your view of Emma change? Do you feel sorry for her because she can't openly be with the one she loves or do you dislike her and think what she is doing to Charles is wrong?
    • Do you think that Emma's attitude toward country life is sympathetic at all? How about her attitude towards the members of her family, especially Charles and Berthe? How does the synthesis of these two attitudes make you as the reader feel about Emma as a person?
    • Do your past experiences or thoughts and interpretations of other works influence your interpretation or expectations of this work?
    • Does anyone in your life remind you of Emma? Why?
    • Instead of judging Emma for her marital infidelity and apathetic attitude for accumulating debt, put yourself in her shoes. For the lackluster, unexciting treatment given to her by Bovary and the other men's lustful attraction to her, is it truly wrong for her to act this way? Would it be surprising for any of us to act in this same manner? In any underlying way, isn't Flaubert giving an excuse for her actions by showing the lackluster treatment from the other citizens?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • What significance do you think nature has had on Madame Bovary? Does she yearn for nature like the romantic poets or during the Enlightenment period? Does the fact that she grew up surrounded by nature show anything about how she wanted to live life?
    • Gustave Flaubert is metaphor heavy when it comes to comparing people with animals, farm animals in particular. How does the extended metaphor, people are like farm animals, affect the theme(s) of Madame Bovary?
    • How do Emma's dreams of her perfect romantic environment contrast with her real "dull countryside, imbecile petty bourgeois, life in its ordinariness" environment?

    QUEER THEORY:

    • How does the cross between sex and experience (or inexperience) define Charles, Emma, Leon and Rodophe respectively?
    • If sex hadn't been such a casual thing in these times, would Emma have reached the same fate?
    • Are the affairs Emma participates in reprehensible or justified since her husband could not satisfy her needs?
    • Does anyone else think that the relationship between Emma and her admirers is defined through their sex lives? With Charles being so inexperienced and without love it show how little Emma cares about their relationship working. While she and Rodolphe have a very sexual relationship just because she was so interested in his money and the prospect of living with him on the "good side of life." Do you think Flaubert was provoking the open sexuality of some women and how their relationships were based on the money side of life?
    • How would the promiscuity of Emma while married be viewed or punished by the church compared as compared to if she was not promiscuous but simply in love with another woman instead in that time period? Which would be worse?

    • Give concrete examples (quotes) from the texts to support your arguments, but not to fill up space. These quotations are the evidence you need to provide to prove your thesis. You will usually use at least one in each of body paragraphs.
    • In order to integrate quotes appropriately, first introduce the quote by making the point, connected to your thesis, that the passage illustrates.
