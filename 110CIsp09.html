LEARNING OUTCOMES: At the end of this course, students will be able to:
1. use learned grammatical structures to speak and write in elementary or novice-level French;
2. create written and oral sentences using learned vocabulary;
3. read French at the elementary or novice level;
4. understand spoken French at the elementary or novice level;
5. identify aspects of the cultures of the French-speaking world.

WELCOME TO FRENCH! This course aims at giving you a solid foundation in understanding, speaking, reading and writing the French language at the elementary level. To accomplish these goals, it is essential that we use French as the language of the classroom. Handy phrases for communicating in French:
Répétez, s'il vous plaît. = Repeat, please.
Une minute, s'il vous plaît.= Wait a minute, please.
Comment dit-on [English word] en français? = How do you say [English word] in French.?
Que veut dire [French word]?= What does [French word] mean ?
Qu'est-ce que c'est? = What's that?
Je ne sais pas. = I don't know.
Je ne comprends pas. = I don't understand.

Although the language of instruction and interaction in my classroom is French, I do not expect you to always understand everything I am saying. Please do not hesitate to let me know when you do not understand. I will find another way of getting my point across, whether it is by paraphrasing, elaborating, or simply slowing down.

I will do my best to keep the class atmosphere creative, lively, and accepting of your best efforts. In return, you, too have some responsibilities. Please read carefully and follow the information and advice given below.

HOMEWORK: Each module of the textbook is divided into two parts: 1) White Pages (Thèmes, Pratiques de conversation, Perspectives culturelles, Écoutons ensemble, Lecture, Expression écrite) and 2) Green Pages (Structures utiles)

Before each class, look through the white pages (column 2 of the syllabus) so you know what we will be doing in class. The CDs included with your textbook correspond to sections in the white pages (look for the audio CD graphic). Listening to them will help you get used to hearing French.

The third column in the syllabus lists selected Lecture: reading exercises. There are corresponding written exercises to turn in listed on the syllabus (Compréhension et intégration; Et vous? Avez-vous compris?) I will correct these for you. If you are having trouble with the reading questions, try writing out the question and translating it into English. I'll correct that, too.

Your main homework is to write out the answers to exercises in the green pages (column 4 of the syllabus). You may choose to do the even or odd questions for each exercise. Check this work using Appendix B (page 485 in the textbook). All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. If there are no errors, put a check mark so I know that you've corrected your work. If you have questions about an exercise, write me a note on your homework. Reminder: copying answers from the textbook or workbook answer key without trying them on your own, is not only a waste of time (what would you learn by copying?), but also an Academic Integrity violation (see above). If you have no idea what the exercise wants you to do, it makes sense to contact me or to check the answers to see a model of what is expected, but follow up by doing your own work. Try your best. You will never be graded on the number of errors in your homework or workbook. Mistakes are normal when you are learning.

You will turn in the corrected work from the green pages daily and all other written assignments when indicated on the syllabus. Please put the page numbers at the top of each homework assignment. Full credit will be given only for corrected work turned in on time.

On your own, you will also do the exercises in the Workbook/Lab Manual corresponding to each Module. All exercises should be corrected clearly in a different color. Mark any sections you don't understand so that you can ask about them in class or I can explain when I'm correcting your work. In the first half of the Workbook, you may do even or odd questions in each exercise, but you must do each exercise. You will normally skip Synthèse, as indicated on the Syllabus. In the second half of the Workbook/Lab manual, you will need to do all the questions. Suggested completion dates are indicated on the syllabus. Correct your Workbook/Lab Manual exercises using the WorkbookAnswer Key pdf and the LabManualAnswer Key.pdf. Bring your Workbook/Lab Manual to each exam so that I can give you credit for your work. You will get full credit (10 points) for complete and corrected (in a different color) exercises and partial credit (5 points) for uncorrected exercises or late lab work.

Exercises in the Lab Manual are to be completed with the audio available in the Language Lab and online. See the section Language Lab below.

On your own, you will also read and do the self-correcting exercises in English Grammar for Students of French. Assigned sections for each exam are listed on the Syllabus. A very detailed listing of the sections which correlate to the grammar we are studying in Motifs is listed in the Correlation Guide pdf. The completed and corrected exercises are due each exam day for full credit (5 points).

HOW TO DO WELL IN CLASS:

1) Prepare assignments before coming to class so we can get the most out of our class time together. Assignments are to be completed as indicated on the syllabus. It is nearly impossible to “cram” for this course and do well. Learning a language is like building a house of cards: each one must be well in place before adding the next. I urge you to spend at least 30 minutes on French per day, rather than studying all at once before exams. Regular repetition of small quantities of information is the most successful method for learning a foreign language.

2) Participate actively in each lesson. Mentally answer each and every question when a classmate is responding. Class participation and homework make up 25% of your grade. The more you participate, the better your grade will be, and the better your French will become. Your class grade reflects your performance in class (attendance, knowledge of daily lesson, volunteering answers, asking questions). Take listening comprehension seriously — it will help both your proficiency and your grade (there is a listening component on exams).

3) Take risks. Speak out. Don’t be afraid to make mistakes. I am here to help. The more you speak, the more you will get out of the class. Save perfection for exams. The desire to communicate is the most important consideration when speaking.

4) Speak French as much as possible in the classroom. Speak French outside the classroom whenever possible, too. Listen to French music, go to French movies, eat French food!

5) Bring textbook to class every day. This is essential. You should also purchase a decent quality French/English-English/French dictionary. While not required, it will help you find words not explained in the textbook.

6) Take notes in class whenever appropriate. Buy 3x5 notecards to make personalized verb charts and other study aids. Study with a friend outside of class.

I look forward to a pleasant and productive semester! Don't hesitate to ask me for help and assistance in person or by email.

My personal teaching philosophy is reflected in this quotation from educator Haim Ginnott (1922-1973):
“It’s my personal approach that creates the climate. It’s my daily mood that makes the weather. As a teacher, I possess a tremendous power to make a student’s life miserable or joyous. I can be a tool of torture or an instrument of inspiration. I can humiliate or humor, hurt or heal. In all situations, it is my response that decides whether a crisis will be escalated or deescalated and a student humanized or dehumanized.”
