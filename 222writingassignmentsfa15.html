Writing assignments:

  • Write a summary of Chapter 7 in A Short Guide to turn in.
  • Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Part 3?
  • Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

    FORMALIST CRITICISM:

    • Emma Bovary is in a constant state of switching back and forth between being a wild woman that dreams of running off with men and enjoying the finer things in life, and then returning to be a woman of the household and begins to put an emphasis on living a Godly life. From the time we are introduced to Emma, we know she was at a convent, married to Charles but wanted to live in a world of finer things, falls for Leon, is abandoned by Leon, goes back to trying to be a somewhat of a good wife, falls for Rodolphe and spends money on finer things, is abandoned by Rodolphe, goes to a life of living Godly, and again falls for Leon and begins to indulge in spending more money than before. Emma’s character would suggest that she is simply trying to find happiness, happiness that will brighten up her so-called dull life. But even if Emma gets her happiness, she must always go through a period of depression. The form of Madame Bovary is mainly about Emma going back and forth but always ending in the same result. So is all of this flip-flopping in Emma’s character the single most defining aspect of this story? Or is there another aspect(s) that would better define this story?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • How does Emma's extravagant personality contribute to her hypocritical classism and socioeconomic status in Madame Bovary?
    • When you first meet someone, what is the first thing you evaluate (the person's face, eyes, clothes, shoes, etc.)? In the story, when two people meet they usually evaluate the other's fingernails. What was so revealing about fingernails in regards to social class, profession, or wealth? What does this reveal regarding the priorities of society during that time? 

    Feminist Criticism:

    • Emma is constantly burdened with the gender injustices of her time. In the story, she desperately wishes for a son so that her child will have the freedoms she never could. One could argue that this desire is perhaps the most prominent maternal moment that she has throughout the story regarding her child. Do you think that if she had gotten her wish and birthed a son, she would have been more devoted to her role of motherhood or rejected it just the same simply because it is a symbol of her set role and marriage? Do you think, had Emma had a son, she would have the potential to turn into Old Madame Bovary, who lives vicariously Charles?
    • Emma is seen as a dramatic person in this story. Do you think this is because during this time period females were not really allowed to do anything without permission of men? Is it possible that if Emma was transported into this time period that she would ultimately become a different person do to the fact that she would have more to do? Do you think Charles ignores her unhappiness due the fact that Emma is female?
    • Does Flaubert's depiction of Emma's choices and behavior of striving to be a "lady" possibly reflect his views of women or humanity in general? (Taking into account her adulterous behavior and the fact that she, by trying to achieve for herself the things she wants, actually strays away from proper "ladylike" behavior)
    • Often in the novel it is evident that Emma has more ambition than Charles and wishes him to have more ambition (The Hippolyte incident). While Charles is meek, foolish, and underspoken, Emma is opinionated, full of aspirations and is one to take initiative. Charles takes on more feminine qualities while Emma takes on more masculine ones. What statement is Flaubert possibly trying to make here regarding women's roles in marriage and society?
    • If Emma had married someone like Leon or Rodolphe, do you think she would have been happy and well? Or is her sickness and spending her way of asserting herself and drawing attention to herself in a male-dominated time period?
    • According to Emma, she is "powerless" being a woman. Is she truly powerless? Do her actions reflect something different? Does the era she is living in influence her opinions of her seemingly impossible situations? How does Flaubert portray her?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • As we are all aware, there is quite a bit of irony and sarcasm by Flaubert in Madame Bovary. However we are aware of this because we know what period this novel was written in. If someone was unaware it was written in a realist time period, would they think this is a romantic novel? Do you think Flaubert wrote this with intentions of people to read it hundreds of years later and analyze the differences between realism and romanticism? What would you say are the greatest factors to recognize that this novel was written during the realist period?
    • What major leaders or celebrities of the time period may have influenced Flaubert's Madame Bovary?
    • Is the idea of marriage more or less unbreakable and sacred today than it was when marriage vows could not be broken (until death do us part)?
    • Before reading this story, we were told that Flaubert had originally wanted to write romantic pieces of work, but his friends mocked him a lot for it, causing him to change directions and write a realist piece; however, do you think that characters like Emma and Leon, who are clearly meant more for the romantic period, are his way of incorporating his original desire? Do you think he can relate most to them because that's the kind of writing he initially wanted to write?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Madame Bovary's outlook on life shifted immensely when she began reading romance novels at the convent. From then on she is constantly yearning for a more romantic life--mostly revolving around her relationships. Is this Flaubert's way of commenting on the psychological desires humans have? Do you believe that Madame Bovary is conscious of her desires for a romantic lifestyle?
    • Psychological Criticism
      While reading fiction stories, I have always wondered if the plot of the story is impacted and formed around the author's personal life. After contemplating, I concluded that the plot almost has to be connected to the author's experiences in one way or another, whether it is consciously or unconsciously. After reading about the personal life of Flaubert, I found out that he never got married. Do you think his shame and frustration of not finding "the one" is portrayed through Emma's romantic frustrations? If so, how? [http://www.online-literature.com/gustave-flaubert/]

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • How did you respond to the character of Emma Bovary? Do you think Flaubert intended her to be sympathetic at all? Would she garner more sympathy in a modern setting where she would surely be diagnosed with a mental disorder such as bipolar disorder or where her consumerist ways are more commonplace?
    • Do you feel a connection to any of the characters in Madame Bovary? Which character? Do you believe this affects your interpretation of the story? How?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • The story takes place in the French countryside which makes it impossible for Charles to stay at home and see all of his clients. Would the affair with Rodolphe have happened if Charles had lived in a city where his clients only came to see him?

    QUEER THEORY:

    • "How does the concept of heteronormativity (the idea that all characters are considered heterosexual until stated otherwise) impact the story in regards to who is attracted to Emma, or vice-versa?"

    Paper Topics

    A. Critical creative rewriting

    You become the author/artist. Take one of your favorite stories, poems or plays and imagine what happens to the characters after the story ends. You could also rewrite the story before it starts or focus on an important scene from a different character's point of view: Cunégonde's, Berthe's or Gerasim's… One student rewrote Pope's Essay on Man in the format of Baudelaire's "Enivirez-vous." You could also write a letter from one character to another in the same story or a different story. For example: What advice would Rodolphe give Ashenbach? For a model, see Ben Franklin's letter of "Advice on Choosing a Mistress".

    Pay attention to the author's narrative style, plot, characterization, vocabulary and imagery, so that you can imitate it (or even make a parody of it). If you use direct quotes from the original text, put these passages in italics. Include the page reference for your quote. Do not include historical anachronisms, such as a telephone in Madame Bovary or snooze button on Gregor's alarm clock.

    The creative part of the paper should be two to three pages long.

    The second half of the paper will be the critical portion: a writing journal or commentary. It should also be a minimum of three pages long for the final version. Explain the stages of your project: first, the choice of story or character, next, the problems and reflections you had while rewriting, including all the ideas you came up with and rejected and the reasons why. Some of the other ideas may lead to other projects later and it is good to have a record of them. Finally, include what you learned about your author and text and the process of writing. For example, what did you notice about your author's style when you read the story for class? What did you notice for the first time during or after class? How did your observation or appreciation of his/her style change as you worked on imitating it? For specifics, be sure to mention the criteria listed here. The writing journal portion of this paper is one of the rare occasions in a college paper where it is appropriate to use "I," as you recount your experience as a writer.

    In other words, your rewrite is your response to a text. Your commentary is the reflection on that response. For the peer review draft, you should include a page of your rewriting and a page of commentary, perhaps an outline. You will not have a thesis, per se, but you should have a very clear story idea or premise for alternative art project. Date entries in your writer's journal. Each time you get new input, such as your peer reviews and your instructor review, revise your creative section, and add to the journal section to reflect the new issues raised by the reviewers.
