    Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Wednesday, February 15

    Finish reading Madame Bovary 1215-1249.

    Writing assignments

    1. Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Book III?
    2. Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also
    3. inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

    FORMALIST CRITICISM:

    • Flaubert uses irony to show different ideas and attitudes the characters have. Give an example and explain its significance to the story.
    • Emma is an anti-hero. An anti-hero is a hero with a flaw. What are some of Emma's flaws? Which flaw is the biggest/worst one?
    • How is the character Homais tied into reinforcing the overall meaning intended by Flaubert in Madame Bovary?
    • How do the characters Emma, Charles, Léon, etc form and define the meaning of Madame Bovary?
    • How does the plot create meaning in Madame Bovary?
    • How do the different points of view relate to the organization of Madame Bovary?
    • In Chapter 1 of Part III, Emma tears up her letter on the carriage ride. What do you think this represents?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • In Madame Bovary, are the characters aware of their different places/classes in society, how is it presented in their work and everyday life?
    • Charles and Emma were both originally raised in two completely different social and economical classes. How does this affect the characters' lives later in the story when they are married?
    • What do you think Madame Bovary says about the values of the 1850s? Does this relate to ideological values and the economic issues present?
    • To Flaubert, bourgeois life was mundane—however, by having satirical characters such as Homais champion the working class, and using his depictions of various peasant characters in Madame Bovary, can one draw a conclusion that he detested the proletariat as well? Or are the crude depictions of the peasant class more satirical of the social climate created by the bourgeoisie (and therefore made more ironic by a character like Homais singing the praises of the proletariat)?
    • Much of the end of the story of Madame Bovary is centered on Emma Bovary's increasing debts and loans. How does the author criticize the economic system: Emma has to borrow money and has to keep borrowing money to pay the debts that she cannot repay?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • How are the lives of women and the lives of men portrayed in this time period? Compare them to how they are portrayed today.
    • What are the relationships and differences between females in Madame Bovary? For example, consider the actions/character of Emma in comparison to her mother-in-law and Madame Homais?
    • Do you feel that Emma strives to be a man ( ie break away from womanly duties) or that she is used to being "tied down" and is just trying to rebel against society?
    • It is clear that there is a distinction between how women's and men's lives are lived in Madame Bovary. You can see this when Emma wants a boy rather than a girl when she is pregnant because she knows the boy would be able to live a better life. She even says, while talking to Léon, "One can't afford to be self-indulgent if one has a thousand things to attend to." Léon responds, "Oh, I can imagine. . ." and Emma says, "No, you can't; you're not a woman." Showing Emma's moral destruction, is Flaubert saying it is her fault because she is the one who chose to have affairs, she is the one that go herself and family into debt, she is the one who could have avoided all of this? Or is he showing how women of the 19th century felt trapped? That she had no choice, she wanted to be happy and fulfill her life and that was the only thing that could do it for her? Since she was basically only seen as an object of sexuality and beauty to all these men, was it good or bad that she took advantage of it? Or was it the men and the period's time for only giving her that choice?
    • Describe the role of women in the novel? What can women in the novel do to make a living? List examples from the text.
    • When looking at two female characters of Madame Bovary, Emma Bovary and Madame Homais, what attributes and characteristics do the women display to support or oppose the feminine role? How does each of these characters view the stereotyical position women were suppoed to fit into during this time period?
    • Does Emma Bovary represent an ideal wife? Why or why not and what characteristics or examples clarify this?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • Throughout Madame Bovary, elements of a changing society can be seen through the blend of different characters. How did the periods of the Enlightenment, Romanticism, Realism and the French Revolution help to shape the characters and the plot of Madame Bovary and what did Gustave Flaubert think of each of these periods?
    • How is Emma's tragedy a representation of a failed attempt of the new European middle class to join the elite in society?
    • How does the plot of the novel represent the social situation and family dynamics of the time? List examples.
    • How was Flaubert mocking Romanticism through his work Madame Bovary?
    • How do you think Madame Bovary reflects the time period's view towards women?
    • What do you think would happen to Emma if she were to run off Rodolphe? How would the society in the Realist period would react to this?
    • If Emma really wanted to leave Charles, what are some ways that she could do this in the Realist Period? How would the society deal with divorces during the period?
    • How does Flaubert address the different classes in the period? What are some examples that were included to point out the characteristics/separation between each class?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Why do you think the author adds a character such as Rodolphe to the story? What does his personality say about wealthy men in these times?
    • We know that Flaubert wrote Madame Bovary to show that people can be foolish and live in fantasy rather than the real world. He does this by portrarying the characters as fools and making us think the same way, but he also makes us feel bad for them as well. Can it be that he was going through the same thing Emma was, showing his alter ego?
    • LIst situations from the novel that reflect the personality and character of Emma. What do they say about her personality?
    • There are many psychological illnesses illustrated in Madame Bovary by Gustave Flaubert. What signs of these illnesses are found in the text and what do they say about the characters' psychological state?
    • How does Charles' naïveté play a role in Emma's infidelities?
    • What behavior and emotions does Madame Bovary display that reveal she is depressed and unhappy?
    • What is revealed about Charles' personality when he is so ignorant of reality or the truth?
    • It seems that throughout the novel Emme is unsure of what she really wants. She seems like she just wants a life of love, however, she finds that she actually wants more. She also wants a child, but is unhappy when it is a girl. Last, when her daughter seeks her mother, Emma is unable to accept her daughter, but somehow seems to show some sympathy after her daughter is hurt. These all can be signs of a psychological disorder. Do you think she shows signs of depression or bipolar disorder? Why do you think she cannot find a place in her life where she is truly happy? Explain.

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • How do your past relationships with the opposite sex affect how you read and interpret this text? Who is it that you are sympathizing more with, Charles or Emma?
    • How do you think that Flaubert's past persuaded him to write Madame Bovary? What did you see that made you think that?
    • In Madame Bovary, did you find any underlying meaning to the overall plot? Was there anything the author wanted the reader to take out of the reading?
    • What is your response to marrying for love and/or money? Are love and money mutually exclusive, especially with Valentine's Day mostly being about buying gifts?
    • Who do you think was Flabuert's intended audience for Madame Bovary? Was it the Youth (20-30 year olds) or an older crowd? Do you think this was a risky story speaking of adultery during this time period?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • In what way(s) does Emma's environment contribute to her desires?
    • On page 1195, in the 6th paragraph, there is clear imagery of realism (factory, smoke, etc). What roles, if any, do the natural images (wind, great black fish, etc.) play?
    • How do the environmental elements that shape the different settings in Madame Bovary affect the characters within these different settings? For example, countryside vs city.

    QUEER THEORY:

    • Based on Emma's sexual tendencies to give her life some excitement, could it have been possible for Emma to be bisexual if the opportunity arose?
    • Is Emma permanently messed up or can her wild lifestyle be fixed?
    • How do Emma's relationships with men throughout the novel relate to the themes?
    • During Flaubert's work, Madame Bovary is repeatedly unfaithful to Charles. Is this infidelity brought on by her need to find real love and happiness or just Madame's need to be sexually deviant?

    • How does Gregory's transformation relate to the overall theme of the story?

    • How does Gregor's current state of being reflect the pressures caused by work, home, and social obligations and responsibilities?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • How do economic conditions determine the characters' lives in The Metamorphosis?
    • How does the economic situation of Gregor's family relate to his bodily changes and isolation from other people?
    • What social class would Gregory's family be a part of? Does Gregory having to work help you determine their class?
    • Based on the description of Gregor's job and how he was treated by his supervisors, it can be argued that he had always been considered an insect long before his literal transformation (ie a worker bee, an ant, a mindless drone). In other words, was it society that made the insect or was it just him?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • Which woman in this story is empowered?
    • What are the roles of the women in this story?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • How does the cultural context of eastern Europe and the new World War relate to the characteristics of The Metamorphosis?
    • How important is knowing the historical contexts (both the work's and your own) in interpreting the work?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • How does Gregory's voice being unrecognizable by human beings affect his feelings and actions toward the situation?
    • How does Gregory's family's emotions and behaviors change as a result of his transformation?
    • How did Gregory's family change after his transformation into an insect?
    • What does Gregor's transformation reveal about how he thinks about himself?
    • Gregory's life is simple and complicated. He is a workaholic, because he is the only one (he thinks) that can provide for his family. When he wakes up and realizes that he is a monstruous vermin, he doesn't react the way most people would. Instead, he accepts it and begins to explore, something he didn't do when he was human. Is this some sort of alter ego of his?
    • How does Gregory's current state as a monstruous vermin isolated in his bedroom relate to his everyday life as a traveling salesperson?
    • Franz Kafka's stories reflect "heroes" that struggle to "…prevail against a vast, meaningless, and apparently hostile system (Norton p. 1966). In your opinion, how is the transformation of Gregory Samsa into a monstruous vermin related to the hardships that Franz Kafka suffered as a child? In other works, how is Kafka expressing his hardships through the life of Gregory Samsa?
    • How does the work reflect the author's personal psychology?
    • In the first half of the work, how does the transformation of Gregory reflect the author's personal psychology?

    • How do you think Franz's life influenced aspects of The Metamorphosis?

    • How did you respond to the opening scene of the story and what did it set you up to expect from the remainder of the story?

    Two areas that are normally weak in the first draft are the use of quotations and the conclusion.

    • Give concrete examples (quotes) from the texts to support your arguments, but not to fill up space. These quotations are the evidence you need to provide to prove your thesis. You will usually use at least one in each of body paragraphs.
    • In order to integrate quotes appropriately, first introduce the quote by making the point, connected to your thesis, that the passage illustrates.
