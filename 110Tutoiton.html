Subject pronouns: je, me, tu, il, elle, on, nous, vous, ils, elles.
As Tex says: Subject pronouns are subjects of verbs. In French, a subject pronoun is immediately or almost immediately followed by its verb. The use of subject pronouns is mandatory in French; always use a subject pronoun to construct sentences in the absence of a noun subject. Visit this page to learn more and test yourself. Subject pronouns are covered in our textbook on page 29.

Stress/tonic/accentuated/ disjunctive pronouns: moi, toi, lui, elle, soi, nous, vous, eux, elles

As Tex says: Disjunctive pronouns (also known as tonic or stressed pronouns) refer to people whose names have already been mentioned or whose identity is obvious from context. They are used in a variety of situations in French, most often in short answers without verbs, for emphasis, or for contrast with subject pronouns. Visit this page to see when to use them and to test yourself. Disjunctive pronouns are discussed in our textbook on page 148.

Possessive adjectives: ma, mon, mes…
In English, my, your, his, hers, our, your, and their. In French, the possessive adjectives, like all adjectives, agree in gender (male/female) and number (singular/ plural) with the noun they modify. It may seem odd to English speakers that the adjective agrees with the noun it modifies rather than the person who posseses the object, but that's another way French and English are different. Example:
She loves her sweater. Elle adore son pull-over.He gets along well with his sister. Il s'entend bien avec sa sœur.
Possessive adjectives are discussed in our textbook on page 84.
