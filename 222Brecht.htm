Principles: “Verfremdung” = defamiliarization, alienation

Aristotelian Theatre     Epic Theatre

universal, unchanged human nature     humans who are changeable and able to change
want to know the end want to know the process
play grows, develops montage of independent scenes
plotted narrated
linear plot  plot goes in curves, circles

“Verfremdungseffekte” = alienation effects

direct address to the spectators
banners with captions or projected text
commentary: by narrator or through songs
open stage, visible machinery, no curtain

different method of acting:
no identification with character, but move in and out of role
“show” the character, not “be” the character;
actor has distance from role, may step out of the role
possible use of masks

different role of music:
not illustrating, but commenting, contradicting

distant/historic time; broken chronology
episodic structure, scenes, not acts
can have short, subsidiary skits in between scenes
interpolated songs
distant setting/place/culture; exotic, non-realistic or “anywhere”

• Brecht called Der gute Mensch von Sezuan ein Parabelstük--a parable play.

Parable
A short simple story illustrating a moral lesson. In a parable the story is developed not for its own sake but only in so far as it reinforces the moral, which is always explicit. The parables of Christ, such as those of the Good Samaritan and the Prodigal Son, are the most famous of this genre.
Beckson and Ganz, Literary Terms:  A Dictionary.

• Karl Marx
Most famous for his analysis of history in terms of class conflict: "The interests of capitalists and wage-laborers are diametrically opposed to each other.” Human nature is not constant but a result of changing historical conditions.

alienation: Giving up ownership of one's own labor -one's capacity to transform the world-is tantamount to being alienated from one's own nature; a spiritual loss.

Commodity fetishism: we mistakenly think the things we produce are the source of our power and creativity. We should see each other as colleagues in a collective human enterprise, not as isolated individuals alienated from each other.
