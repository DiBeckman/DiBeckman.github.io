Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, Feb. 16
Finish your character sketch and turn in. Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Web CT posts or the idea for Paper 1.

Shannon's 6 types of critical questions (pages 61-64):
Section 1
• FORMALIST CRITICISM:
How do the secondary characters influence story/plot? How does Leon influence Ema's decisions and character?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
  Marxist Criticism: Madame Bovary has numerous social settings with different classes present in each. Also it is apparent that Emma is obsessed with the upper class and wishes desperately to be a part of their exclusive group. Why does Flaubert devote so much of his work to the different descriptions of the social classes? Is there a hidden message here; if so, what is it?

  Feminist Criticism:
• How is Emma's life portrayed in the work? Does the fact that she rejects her role and accepts it on occasion say anything about the oppression of women or if there is justification for her behavior?
•Why do you think Emma is never satisfied? Is Flaubert's discontent wiht his life a parallel to Emma's dissatisfaction wiht love? Does Flaubert overexaggerate Emma's expectations on purpose?
• What is the relationship between men and women in this work and how does their relationship cause or resolve conflict throughout the work?
• Considering the realist them of the story, how does Flaubert portray women, specifically Emma? How does this differ from the way women are portrayed in today's society? Explain whether Emma accepts or rejects this role and give examples supporting your answer.
• Why does Emma have so much power over Charles in a time in history where women don't have that much socioeconomic or political power?
• How are women's lives portrayed in the work. Do they accept or reject these roles?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICSM:
• How does the culture and society of living in a small country town affect Emma? Is this what caused her to be unhappy?
• During the time period of Madame Bovary, was the sanctity of marriage regarded in the same way that it is today? Was it more or less highly regarded?
• What was Flaubert's view on the social structure at the time of the story, and how did he express that in Madame Bovary?
• How does Charles' attitude about marriage and adultery reflect the time in which the story was written?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
• What was the overal mood that could be tied together by all of the characters' personalities and characteristics. Could the author have intended for this novel to represent his personal life?
• Madame Bovary was always dreaming of the classy life that she never got. She was always trying to associate herself with other people of the upper class. Even after she got married, she still did not realize the reality. What is strange is that she had a child. Usually people who have children are busy taking care of them. What happened to her maternal instincts? Why didn't that help her snap out of her dreams and bring her to reality?
• Do the dreams and desires of the main character Emma reveal anything to us about the dreams and desires of Flaubert or someone close to him? Do her actions reflect his or someone close to him?
• What view does Flaubert want readers to have of Madame Bovary, given that there are three characters who share that name. What mindset does Flaubert want the readers to develop of this character?
• Why is it so hard for Emma to accept her situation and why does she have to ruin things for everyone else, especially her daughter?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:
•Which character do you find it hardest to relate to? Why?
•What was your reaction to Leon's open flirtation with Emma when they meet?
• Emma believes that she can in some way change the way the world is. She thinks that every new thing that comes into her life will make her happy. Why is this unrealistic? How are Emma's actions and decisions affected by her needs and views on life?

Section 2
• FORMALIST CRITICISM:
• How does Emma's suicide, and the consequential end it puts to her cycle of hope and misery, play a role in the overall meaning and lesson of the novel?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
  Marxist Criticism:
• How do economic conditions determine the characters' lives?
• How does Emma's dependence on credit influence her actions, especially towards Charles?
• What motivations or reasons does L'Heureux have to allow Emma to dig her own financial (and literal) grave? How do these reasons reflect Romanticism and Realism?
• In Madame Bovary, are upper class women or male servants in a better social class? How does Flaubert show this?

  Feminist Criticism:
• What does the fact that Emma wants a baby boy show about women's role in society in this book and during the time that it was written?
• How is Emma Bovary viewed by the townspeople when they suspect her of being unfaithful?
• Is Emma's perception of women's roles in society similar to other women in the novel? in her historical time period?
• How does Emma feel about the traditional roles of women? Does she feel trapped or does she use this to her advantage?
• How do men and women reveal their feelings differently in Madame Bovary? (Does this correspond to your personal experience?)
• How does Emma's behavior compare to the other women in the novel? Is their behavior akin to the expectations of that time?
• How does Madame Bovary's role as mother fall within the lines of feminism while either strengthening the feminist view or devaluing it?
• How are today's women different from the women in Madame Bovary's time?
• In which incidents in Madame Bovary does Flaubert show an understanding of the hardships of women in this time period?

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICSM:
• Why does Flaubert have a Romantic character in a Realist novel?
• How historically accurate is the novel?
• How do social considerations play an important role in courtship in Madame Bovary?
• What are the reasons that Emma desires a male child?
• How did Flaubert utilize the themes and views of the Enlightenment, Romantic, and Realist periods to shape his characters?
• Did Flaubert's use of the science vs. religion debate shape the characters in strong ways? How so?
• How does the work reflect the period it represents?

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
• Emma seems to have trouble making the right decision throughout the novel. What was the movitation behind her actions? Was she destroying her life and the lives of those around her intentionally or were her actions inadvertent?
• Is Charles' first marriage the reason he felt so strongly for Emma or would he feel that way even without that bad experience? How does Emma's experience in the convent shape the way she acts in life and towards Charles?
•Would Emma truly have been happy in the long run had she in fact eloped with either Leon or Rodolphe?
• Is it possible for Emma to be happy in the long run at all?
• Did Flaubert possibly have bad relationships with women in his past that caused him to share Emma's character the way he did?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:
•What is the sound out in the forest on page 1133?
• How would someone react toward this novel, and Emma in particular, if they were from an impoverished lifestyle in comparison to someone who currently lives the way Emma dreams of living?
• Do you sympathize with Emma, a Romantic stuck in a very realist situation, or the character of Charles, who is happy only because he is so naive, more deserving of our sympathy?

For two possible explanations for Marcel's behavior,
1) Read about separation/anxiety disorder:
http://www.mentalhealth.samhsa.gov/disasterrelief/publications/allpubs/CA-0007/default.asp

2) Also: "The Oedipus complex in Freudian psychoanalysis refers to stage of psychosexual development in childhood where children of both sexes regard their father as an adversary and competitor for the exclusive love of their mother. The name of the complex derives from the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unwittingly kills his father Laius and marries his mother Jocasta.

Freud considered the successful resolution of the Oedipus complex to be key to the development of gender roles and identity, with boys and girls resolving the conflicts differently as a result of castration anxiety and penis envy. Freud also held that the unsuccessful resolution of the Oedipus complex could result in neurosis and homosexuality.… Although common usage refers to 'suffering from an Oedipus complex', psychoanalysis does not consider the complex a pathology, but instead a perfectly normal stage that all children go through. Oedipal desires are thought to remain heavily repressed and unconsciously in the minds of all functioning adults."
From wikipedia http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oedipus_complex
