Here are two additional sets of critical questions you may choose to address. They were written by a former student, Garry Pigg (Fall, 2005)

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM

1. How do the environmental/ecological conditions within the setting of the work affect the language, mood, tone, and theme?
2. How does the use of the word "nature" in the work reflect the current scientific beliefs of the time and how does that affect the characters, plot, themes?
3. How does the relationship between man/woman and his/her environment--economically, agriculturally, botanically, socially--affect the characters, language, theme, plot, and tone? i.e. do the characters have to hunt and gather, farm, trade, live in city, country?
4. How do plants, animals, and their conditions in the environment affect the character's livelihood, the plot, the author's tone, the moods, the theme? i.e. the effects of individual species such as cows, camels, horses,goats, bananas, sugar, cotton, corn, potatoes, fungi, bacteria,
viruses; and ambient conditions such as drought, flooding, pollution, climate change, introduction of invasive species, and famine.

QUEER THEORY

1. How do the cultural feelings about sex affect the characters, plot, and themes?
2. How does sexual identity affect the characters? How do they deal with others and their own sexual deviance or breaking of social norms?
3. How does homosexuality or bisexuality play a role in the characters' lives, the tone, mood, theme, and plot of the work?
4. What role does sexuality play in the work? How does it affect the use of language? Does it represent love and beauty or marriage and
reproduction?
5. How does religion affect the tendencies of the characters to express or not express themselves sexually?
6. How does the work represent sex and sexuality? Does it use sexuality to define "others"? Does discussion of sex in the work hinder or help feminism? Does it hurt or help racism, discrimination in general, and civil rights? What is the connection between sexuality, race, gender, and social class in the work(s)?

Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, Sept. 28
Finish your character sketch and turn in. Choose any one of the following questions about Madame Bovary to answer. Include the question, please, with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Web CT posts or the idea for Paper 1.

Shannon's 6 types of critical questions (pages 61-64):

Both sections are listed together here.

• FORMALIST CRITICISM:
• How do the elements of the story, in particular, the references to cattle, windows, doors and clothing, reinforce the realist beliefs held by Flaubert?
• How does the way Emma falls for different charactesrs (Leon, Rodolphe) other than her husband reinforce what we already know about her personality form the previous chapters?

• SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM
Marxist Criticism: Steven, Kaitlin
• Although Emma knows Charles can't afford her lavish spending, she continues to waste money. How does this relate to her prior class distinction?
• According to Karl Marx, "money is the root of all evil." How do you think this applies to Madame Bovary in reference to the outcome of Charles and Emma?
• Is the portrayal of Emma as a spoiled brat a way in which the class structure is criticized?
|
Feminist Criticism:
• Emma's relationship with Léon is a source of conflict, but also a source of resolution. How so?
• Gender roles are important in Madame Bovary. Do you feel that Emma is miserable all the time because she is female or it because society has forced women to think and feel in Emma's way?
• How is Emma's role as a woman portrayed in the novel? Does it change throughout and if so, do you think any of the other characters influence this feminine role?
• What are the differences/similarities of how women are represented in Candide and Madame Bovary?
• Why does Emma Bovary want to wear man-like clothes? What does this mean?
• There are many reasons why Emma rejects her gender role in terms of work, character, expectations. What is the main factor why Emma rejects the role of women in the story and why?
• How are women's lives portrayed in the work? Do the women accept or reject these roles? ;
• Compare and contrast Emma's real life to the fantasy life that she dreams of. How is her life affected by the roles of women as portrayed by the romantic style of literature?
• How does Emma Bovary play her role as a wife, a mother, and a friend to the other characters in the story. How do the other characters respond to her actions and how could this affect her social life?
• What motivations characterize the relationships between men and women in Madame Bovary? eg dominance, power, submission, pleasure, monetary gain, charity, escape, survival, etc.
• Why does it seem that women are the basis of all the problems in this novel??? (ie complaining, breaking up of marriages, etc.)

• CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICSM:
• Between the 18th and 19th centuries, there was a huge cultural change in France. The Revolution and age of revolt and rebellion were over. With culture changing, literature followed. Gustave Flaubert himself changes from a romantic to a realist. One of his best examples of realism is Madame Bovary. What examples in Madame Bovary show the realism and do you think Flaubert brings a romantic double agent to the story? If so, who and why?
• What are Flaubert's views on the church clergy as defined by Abbé Bournisien's character throughout Madame Bovary?
• In the case of the pharmacist Homais, he had no license to practice medicine. So do you believe this is a very common thing among people of this time period not to check a person's credentials out before receiving treatment?
• Why does Flaubert repeatedly use animals to describe human behavior? Is it historical or cultural to compare nature to human behavior?
• Does Emma's behavior differ from that of a 21st century woman? What specific aspects of Flaubert's Madame Bovary were representative of the time in which it was written (1856)? For example, you could look at the importance of class, the relationship between husband and wife, and the roles of other minor characters (such as the priest) as pertaining to that time frame in relation to how they were described in the text?
• Why did Flaubert attack the new middle class (bourgeois) in Madame Bovary and make fun of their incompetence and their lack of taste?
• What was the common attitude toward adultery during this time period? I can remember reading The Scarlet Letter in which it was a horrible crime. Did this story strike a lot of controversy in that respect?
• Does Gustave Flaubert accurately depict 19th century France in Madame Bovary or are there faults within the novel?
• The middle 19th century in France was a time of industrial revolution and the rise of the bourgeoisie. What characters and aspects of Madame Bovary reflect middle 19th century in France with respect to industrialization and the rise of the bourgeoisie?
• Although Madame Bovary is thought of as a realist novel, is this really a realist novel intended for the vast majority or is it more of a satire or even an antithesis to romanticism intended for people critical of romantic works?
Justin

• PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
• How do Emma's dreams and desires for a more fulfilling and exciting life reflect the dreams and desires of Flaubert? Do Emma's internal wishes reflect the wishes of Flaubert?
• What does Emma's character, her behavior, and emotions reveal about her psychological state? Describe her personality in detail as well as how her attitude reflects her psychological state. What does Emma's character reflect about Flaubert?
• What psychological factors influenced emma when she first met Rodolphe?, If personality is defined as the organized pattern of behavioral characteristics of the individual, how would you describe Emma's?
• Why does Emma get so much joy out of being sad? Why can't she ever truly be happy? What does she really want from life? What are her real dreams?
• How does Emma's affair with Leon affect her mood and feelings towards her marriage with Charles?
• Throughout Madame Bovary Emma was consumed by a fantasy world of romanticism. Nothing was ever good enough for her. Does this show Emma's weak psychological state? If so, was there anything the other characters could have done to help her?
• What do Emma's actions, emotions, and behavior towards her child and Charles tell the reader about her psychological state? Could it foreshadow later events in the story?
• Emma Bovary is subject to the decisions of many people around her. First her father persuades her to marry a man she doesn't love, and still later she is subjected to rules of society. Do you think that Emma's mistakes or bad decisions are a product of the effect that these factors have on her, or is she just acting selfishly?

• READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:
Flaubert makes it clear that he is a realist author. Do you feel that he portrays his realism throughout Madame Bovary? Does it change into other forms of writing such as romanticism?
• Do you think Charles know what is going on between his wife, Emma, and other men? What point do you think Flaubert was trying to get across to his readers?
• How do you respond to the ups, down, and numerous blindsiding aspects of the novel and what are the influencing factors in this outlook?
• Flaubert provides a sense of insecurity through Emma's character. Is this insecurity a reflection of Flaubert's feelings or experiences in life? What is Flaubert thinking when he continues to make Emma's life a miserable happiness?
• Flaubert repeatedly uses references to animals to refer to the fallacies that he sees in his characters, whether it be in the name of the novel itself or whether he is describing characters to have characteristics like horses, pigs, and other "lower" animals. Was this from a personal bias or just from the style of writing in the ear? What is Flaubert's view on evolution and the intelligence of non-humans?
Jihad

• ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:
• If Emma is truly in pursuit of a happy life as defined by wealth, why does she choose to move to Yonville, a town known more for its dead than its living?
• How do we see Emma's language, mood, tone and them change, as she makes her way from her father's farm to living with Charles in Tostes and then on to Yonville-l'Abbaye?

• QUEER THEORY:
• Emma tries to run off more than one time to be with someone other than Charles, each time willing to take or leave Berthe. Does Emma's need for sexual approval eventually hurt Berthe the most?
• How far does sex and sexuality affect Emma's life? In what ways does she use her sexuality to her advantage and in what ways is she a victim to her sexuality?
• Why do you think that idea of sex (writing or discussion) has changed so dramatically since the time period that Madame Bovary was written? Why is our generation so much more open or comfortable with sex? Could the answer be attributed to exposure from writers like Flaubert?
• Is there any heterosexual relationship in the novel where both persons are equals?

Backstory that will be helpful as you read Act I of the play:

The Father watches over them, but eventually they move away; the stepfather dies, and the wife and three children move back to the original town without telling the Father. Mother and Stepdaughter take jobs with a dressmaker, Madame Pace, who runs a call-girl service: Mother as a seamstress, Stepdaughter as a prostitute, who is ultimately hired by the Father. 2) Also: "The Oedipus complex in Freudian psychoanalysis refers to stage of psychosexual development in childhood where children of both sexes regard their father as an adversary and competitor for the exclusive love of their mother. The name of the complex derives from the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unwittingly kills his father Laius and marries his mother Jocasta.

Freud considered the successful resolution of the Oedipus complex to be key to the development of gender roles and identity, with boys and girls resolving the conflicts differently as a result of castration anxiety and penis envy. Freud also held that the unsuccessful resolution of the Oedipus complex could result in neurosis and homosexuality.… Although common usage refers to 'suffering from an Oedipus complex', psychoanalysis does not consider the complex a pathology, but instead a perfectly normal stage that all children go through. Oedipal desires are thought to remain heavily repressed and unconsciously in the minds of all functioning adults."
