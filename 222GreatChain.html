The Great Chain of Being

A world view which dominated Western thought from Plato’s time until the Romantic movement.

Three principles:
1) Plenitude. The universe is absolutely full of every possible kind and variety of life; no conceivable species of being remain unrealized.

2) Continuity. Each species differs from the next by the least possible degree, and so merges all but imperceptibly into its nearest related kinds.

3) Gradation. The existing species exhibit a hierarchy of status and so compose a great chain, or ladder, of being extending from the lowest condition of merest existence up to God Himself.

Based on these concepts, Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz (1646-1716) and others develop the doctrine of philosophical optimism: this is the best of all possible worlds that is logicially capable of being brought into existence.

(M. H. Abrams, A Glossary of Literary Terms, 4th ed.)
