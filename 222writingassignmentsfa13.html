Class 16 (Flaubert 5) due Friday, September 27

Finish reading Madame Bovary 1215-1249.

Writing assignments:

  1. Finish your character sketch to turn in. What happens to your character(s) in Book III?
  2. Choose any one of the following questions to answer and include the question with your answer. These questions would also inspire some good discussions in your Moodle posts.

    FORMALIST CRITICISM:

    • Flaubert is obsessed with finding the perfect word to go in his writing. Thus, every word has a purpose. The characters in Madame Bovary are not only individuals, but also society as a whole. How does Flaubert use imagery to develop his characters and his symbols?
    • Does the pace of Madame Bovary reflect Emma's emotions? I noticed that when Emma is bored, melancholy, or unhappy in general, the story seems to drag on, but when she finds passion and excitement, the pace of the story picks up. Do you think Flaubert did this on purpose? Am I the only one who noticed this?
    • We know Flaubert to be particular in his wording(s) and careful to use perfection in his writing. Do we see this practice throughout each book of Madame Bovary? Does he do this with each character description? If not, why might that be?

    SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    Marxist Criticism:

    • Can Emma’s corruption be attributed to the materialistic bourgeois? Or was her it her upbringing in the convent?
    • Throughout the novel, there are stark comparisons between the poor and the rich in France's society. The clear class differences are apparent to Emma, who, although not necessarily poor but also not rich, attempts to move up in class by buying "high-class" and "high fashion" things. This, ultimately, leads to her financial ruin. Do you think that the distinct divides between classes from material possessions was the driving force behind why Emma wasn't satisfied with what she had and tried to change her position in society? Why or why not? 
    • How much does Madame Bovary's reliance on money say about her? How much does the role of the debt play on her fate in the end?

    Feminist Criticism:

    • Does Madame Bovary present women in a positive or negative light? Specifically, is Emma's affair a symbol of female empowerment or the inevitable weakness of women?
    • Why do you think the author portrays Emma in such a negative light? Do you think this is an accurate portrayal of how people view women... as rash, unstable, and manipulative?

    CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

    • How is Napoleon's influence on French culture portrayed throughout Madame Bovary?
    • What in society caused Flaubert to write about such a normal, boring topic and what political movement, if any, motivated Flaubert to write about the middle class?
    • In Madame Bovary, Flaubert gives us enormous details of the French high class society. Was Flaubert criticizing or praising this high-class society?
    • How does this book reflect the period of society in which it was created?
    • Did the rise in Liberalism and ideas about Evolution impact Flaubert's characters in Madame Bovary at the time? How?
    • In the story although Emma is depressed Flaubert keeps her in her marriage with Charles, is this because the time period divorce wasn't as common, the birth of their daughter, or a bit of both?

    PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Emma Bovary is depicted as not truly loving anything except attention and thinking things will solve her problems, such as expensive goods and affairs, even though nothing ever fills the hole within her. Do you think if Madame Bovary existed in the modern era with access to mental health care, such as cognitive-behavioral therapy, her life would have turned out differently? Or do you think she wouldn't have cared, resisting treatment until it was too late?
    • Flaubert goes to a great deal of trouble to paint a picture of Emma's emotional and mental state during the course of Madame Bovary in how she interacts with other characters in addition to how she reacts to varying situations that arise. How do these two factors change in Emma and how does Flaubert use these changes to drive and affect the story's plot?

    READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

    • How did it make you feel when you found out Emma, a supposedly innocent good wife, cheated on her husband, Charles even though he did nothing wrong to her?
    • What values or norms in society is the author ridiculing or making fun of in Madame Bovary? Also, does it seem like the author paints one gender more favorably than the other?

    ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

    • Monsieur Leon puts forward the concept that surrounding and circumstance can affect self-confidence. He notes that in the homes of the wealthy and powerful Parisian, he would be trembling, but on the banks of the Rouen he would be able to dazzle the local folk. Do you think that this view on surrounding is true of all the characters, which characters in particular is the most true for?
    • How do the opposite settings of the countryside, and the city (two places Emma lived in) compare, symbolize, and/or relate to Emma and Charles's relationship?  How does this relate to Emma's emotions and mixed feelings throughout the book?

    QUEER THEORY:

    • Would you agree that this novel contains an immense amount of sexuality? If so, would you agree or consider the fact that Emma may have a serious issue psychologically due to her personal and constant desires and needs for satisfaction in life through men and sex?
    • Do you think the lack of references to sex represents Emma's view of the importance of it in relationships? Does she define their love for each other through physical or emotional means?

FORMALIST CRITICISM:

  • How does Gregory's transformation relate to the overall theme of the story?
  • Is the metamorphosis of the main character what drives the plot of the story?
  • How does Gregor's current state of being reflect the pressures caused by work, home, and social obligations and responsibilities?

SOCIOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

Marxist Criticism:

  • Throughout the first half of Kafka's "The Metamorphosis", there are several references to Gregory's work history and his family's financial state. The concern over money and material possessions is also mentioned all throughout the first half of the story. Do you think that the family's financial situation due to the father's debt has had an effect on how Gregory now views himself and his position as not only a member of the family but also as an individual?
  • How do economic conditions determine the characters' lives in The Metamorphosis?

Feminist Criticism:

  • What does the depiction of the Mother say about women?
  • How do gender roles play a part in the Metamorphosis? How does the way women are treated affect their character development?
  • What is Grete's relationship with Gregor like and how does it change after his transformation?

CULTURAL AND HISTORICAL CRITICISM:

  • How does Kafka convey information about the culture and society of the early 1900’s through his protagonist Gregory Samsa in The Metamorphosis?
  • Traveling salesmen aren't generally considered high on the social scale just as insects aren't high on the food chain. Was Kafka making a statement about how societies views turn inward onto ourselves by making Samsa an insect and becoming disgusted with himself?

PSYCHOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

  • Once Gregor is transformed into a bug his behavior and thoughts are still human. As the story goes his actions and thoughts are that of a bug. Can Gregor's new bug life completely replace his old life to the point he has no human behaviors?  If this happens, can he share a moment with a human, or is this the isolation that Kafka foreshadows?
  • From a psychological perspective, how does the main character’s transformation reflects his deeper concerns? Is this story really a person’s dark subconscious revealing something deeper or has Gregor really changed into a monstruous vermin?
  • Being that Gregor is a loner, how does this psychological state affect his transformation into an insect? Does the loneliness make it easier or harder to be an insect? Is the loneliness something he has felt before, or is it something that arose after transforming into an insect?
  • What do the character's emotions and behavior reveal about their psychological states? What types of personalities are they?
  • What does Gregor's behavior, when his family and boss find out about his metamorphosis, reveal about his desire?
  • Is it possible that The Metamorphosis is a story of Gregory's unconsious mind coming out?
  • Why does Gregor never wish he would turn into a human again?
  • Why does Gregor turn into a monstruous vermin commonly known for being at the bottom of the food chain and not something more regal like say a butterfly or a toucan? Monstruous vermin type bugs are known for being stepped on. Do you think this is why Kafka chose to turn Gregor into a bug? Because all his does is sacrifice to make others happy?

READER RESPONSE CRITICISM:

  • As a reader, we read this piece about the transformation of Gregory and see the world through his many eyes. Because of this, do you believe that we should separate ourselves and be objective when reading, or feel as Gregory would, about the way that his family is treating him?
  • It is evident that Gregor's transformation has a negative effect on his relationship with his family. He views their decision as being very practical and appropriate considering the family. The family members seem to sever Gregor's position in the family. They literally treat him like a bug though he is still himself. He views their decision as being very practical and appropriate considering the family. Do you think their attitude towards him is justifiable? How would you have reacted if you were in the family's position?
  • How does the reader respond to the ideal of humanity? Is Gregor still a human after the transformation or is he just a bug?
  • What does Metamorphosis insinuate about humanity? More specifically, what does Kafka attempt to say about what makes someone human or inhuman? Where is the line drawn?
  • Why does Gregor accept his transformation?

ECOLOGICAL CRITICISM:

  • Does the lack of setting identification and explanation of the environment affect the reader's comprehension of the story? Why or why not?

QUEER THEORY:

  • How does the way Gregory's family treats him after he becomes a vermin similar to the way family and friends react to someone after they admit to being homosexual?
